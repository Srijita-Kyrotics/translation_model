Bombay Rent, Hotel and Accommodation Rate Regulation Act, 1947, Section 28 - Sub-lease by tenant in contravention of lease term - Landlord's suit against tenant and sub-tenant and compensation for use and possession - Jurisdiction of Small Causes Court. Construction of Section 28 - Implication of sub-tenant, to be effected.
Where the lessee of a flat situated within the city of Bombay had a condition that no sub-letting or re-letting of the premises allotted to the tenant could be allowed without the prior consent of the landlord and the tenant, in violation of this condition paid the flat at sub rent, and the landlord filed a suit against him and the sub-tenants, for the use, possession and compensation of the premises in the Small Causes Court, Bombay, and the sub-lessee claimed that the Small Causes Court had no jurisdiction since he was concerned that the suit was not between the landlord and the tenant or for rent within Section 28 of the Bombay Rents, Regulation of Rates of Hotel Lodging Act, 1947:
held (i) that this suit was clearly for possession and that the claim for damages was a mere incidental claim;
(ii) Section 28 of the Act conferred jurisdiction on the Small Causes Court not only to entertain and try any suit or litigation between the landlord and the tenant for recovery of rent or possession, but also "to deal with any claim or question arising out of this Act or any of its provisions" and Section 28 was wide enough to provide that the plaintiff and the sub-leaseholder were sufficient to consider the question raised therein;
(iii) in any case, even though the sub-leaseholder was not a necessary party - for the case he was a competent party and such a joining party cannot alter the nature of the case and cannot diminish the importance of a case between the landlord and tenant under section 28.

The judgement of the court was delivered by Justice Das on 10 December 1952.
- This is an appeal filed by special leave from judgment and order dated January 25, 1952 of the Bombay High Court in Civil Revision Application No. 1119 of 1951. It arises out of a case filed in the Small Causes Court at Bombay for the use and occupation of a flat on the third floor of Sunama House, Cumballa Hill, Bombay, for the purpose of eviction and compensation of Rs. 370 per month with effect from November 1, 1947. Small Causes Court under Section 28 of the Bombay Rents, Hotels and Lodging Houses Rate Control Act, 1947 with effect from November 1, 1947, of Rs. 370 per month. The plaintiffs are the trustees of the will of the deceased Framroze ডি.বি. Taraporevala and the owner of Sunama House. The defendants are two in number, namely, the first defendant Mrs. Dinbai K. Lala to whom the said flat was let by the plaintiffs on September 1, 1942, for Rs. 370 per month. And the second defendant is a limited company to whom the first defendant had sub-let the said flat on the same rent with effect from November 16, 1947. The defendants contested the case on various grounds, but the trial court by its judgment dated October 18, 1950, rejected all the applications and passed a decree directing both the defendants to vacate the flat by March 31, 1951, and pay Rs. 3,317-10 -8 per month from November 1, 1947, to July 31, 1948, in respect of the amount of compensation in favour of the first defendant only, and thereafter Rs. 370 per month from August 1, 1948, in addition to the costs of the case, until possession of the land was obtained. The defendants filed an appeal under Section 29 of that Act. In addition to the various applications before the trial court, the defendants, before the appellate bench, moved an additional application, which was not appealed in their written statement, namely, that in so far as it relates to the second defendant, the Small Causes Court had no jurisdiction to try the case. The appellate bench of the Small Causes Court dismissed the appeal with costs. The second defendant thereafter applied to the High Court for an amendment under Section 115 of the Code of Civil Procedure which was dismissed with costs. The second defendant has now filed an appeal before us after obtaining special leave of this court.
The only argument before us is that the Small Causes Court had no jurisdiction to try this case. The relevant parts of Section 28 of the Act are as follows: ---
"Notwithstanding anything contained in any law and notwithstanding anything contained therein, whether by reason of the amount of the claim or otherwise, for the sake of this provision, the suit or proceeding shall not proceed within its jurisdiction,
(a) in Greater Bombay, the Small Causes Court, Bombay,
(AA)........,
(b)..........
The Landlord and Tenant shall have jurisdiction to try and decide any suit or proceeding between them relating to the recovery of rent or possession of any premises to which any provision of this Part applies, and to decide any application made under this Act. To decide any claim or question arising out of this Act or any of its provisions; and no other court shall have jurisdiction to try any such case, proceeding or application or such claim or question. "
It was not disputed that the provisions of Part-II of the Act applied to the property.The contention of the appellant is that against this, the case was not a case between the landlord and the tenant and hence
In so far as it claimed compensation for use and occupancy, it was not a case for recovery of rent and, therefore, Section 28 had no application and the Small Causes Court had no jurisdiction to adjudicate this case. It is necessary to refer to the plaintiff in this case in the light of this application. Framrose ডি.বি. Having determined their rights as trustees of Taraporevala's will as owners of Sunama House the plaintiffs petitioned that the third floor flat be let to the first defendant on the terms printed on the back of the rent bill form as shown and accepted by the first defendant on September 1, 1942 at a rent of approximately 370 rupees per month. Paragraph 5 of the petition sets out one of those conditions, namely, that the tenant shall not allow the allotment, sub-letting or re-letting of the premises without the prior written consent of the landlords. Paragraph 7 refers to the notice given by the plaintiff to the first defendant on October 17, 1946, to vacate the flat at the end of November 1946, on the ground that two beneficiaries were reasonably and truthfully required under the will to use it. Then, after referring to an unsuccessful attempt on the part of the plaintiff to obtain a certificate under section 9 of the Act, the plaintiff referred to a letter dated November 16, 1947, written by the first defendant to the plaintiffs informing them that from that day he had sub-let the flat to the second defendant. Paragraphs 12 and 13 of the petition state that on December 19, 1947, the second defendant sent a cheque for 370 rupees for the rent for the month of November 1947, but the plaintiffs refused to accept it or to recognise the second defendant as a sub-tenant or otherwise as the lawful occupier. It is further alleged that on January 23, 1948, the plaintiff served a notice on both defendants to vacate the premises by February 29, 1948.
the plaintiffs in paragraphs 14 and 15 constitute grounds for eviction, namely, (1) that the payment of the alleged subpar rent by the first defendant to the second defendant was unjust, unlawful and in violation of the terms of the tenancy and
(2) That the plaintiffs were reasonably and truthfully in need of land suitable for the use and occupation of the two beneficiaries. The plaintiffs pray that both the defendants be directed to vacate the premises and both be directed to pay the plaintiff a compensation of Rs 370 per month from November 1, 1947 for the use and occupation of the premises until the premises are released from occupation. The appellant points out that in the petition the plaintiffs refused to recognise it as a lawful occupier as a sub-tenant or otherwise and treated it as a mere intruder without any legal claim to the flat granted on lease and therefore the case in so far as it was one between the plaintiffs and the appellant (the second defendant) cannot be called a case between the landlord and the tenant and in so far as it demanded compensation from him cannot be called a case for recovery of rent. The last part of the dispute need not hold us back for long, as the case was undoubtedly a case for possession of the flat and the claim for compensation was only incidental and incidental to the claim for possession. The jurisdiction to sue for possession would empower the court not only to pass a decree for possession but also to direct payment of the intermediate benefit until the possession was granted. Such a direction for payment of the intermediate benefit is generally an integral part of the decree for possession. The only question for consideration is, therefore, whether the case was between the landlord and the tenant.
Defendants (plaintiffs) do not claim, as defined in section 5 (11) of the Act, that the appellant (second defendant) is a "tenant." On the other hand the appellant cannot deny that, between the plaintiff and the first defendant, the case is a case between the landlord and the tenant and as such under section 28 of the Act the Small Causes Court is the only court competent to entertain the case. Section 28 Only between a landlord and a tenant
The Tenant does not grant the Small Causes Court jurisdiction to entertain and adjudicate any suit or judgment relating to the recovery of rent or possession of the premises but also to "deal with any claim or question arising out of this Act or any of its provisions." There is no reason to hold that "any claim or question" must be one between the landlord and the tenant. In any case, once there is a case between the landlord and the tenant relating to the recovery of rent or possession of the premises, the Small Causes Court acquires jurisdiction not only to entertain that case but also to "deal with any claim or question arising outside the Act or any of its provisions" which may properly arise in such a case. The plaintiffs in this case have claimed that the alleged payment of sub-rent by the first defendant to the second defendant was unlawful both because it was a breach of the tenancy terms and because the first defendant, as a statutory tenant, is not entitled to create a sub-tenancy after the contractual tenancy has been determined and because they question the validity of the second defendant's claim for any protection under the Act. Thus the claim or question of the respective rights of the plaintiff and the second defendant thus raised in the petition certainly arises from the law and the language of section 28 appears to be broad enough to take this into account. In addition to this section, the decree of possession in a case for eviction against a tenant under the common law is binding on that tenant or the person claiming rights through him and can be enforced against a person who was or is not a party to the suit. The non-joining of such a person in the suit does not make the decree any less binding on him. In this sense, therefore, he is not a necessary party to the eviction case against the tenant. However, it is recognised that such a person is the appropriate party to the case so that a decision may be taken in his presence as to whether the lease has been properly determined and the plaintiff who owns the land is entitled to regain possession of the place so that he can see that there is no collusion between the landlord and the tenant.
under which or through which he claims protection under the law, if he is entitled to any. Such a person may be added as a party to the case from the beginning of the case or at a later stage of the case if the court deems it appropriate to do so. The joining of such a competent party cannot change the character of the case and cannot make the case less important between the landlord and the tenant or take it out of section 28 of the Act. Otherwise the retention of this decision will encourage the diversification of the case so that there will be no end of inconvenience and confusion. In our view Chagla, the decision and reasoning of the CJ are substantially correct and this appeal must fail. We therefore dismiss the appeal with costs.
The appeal was rejected.
