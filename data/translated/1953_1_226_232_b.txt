Bombay Rent, Hotel and Accommodation Rate Control Act, 1949, section 28 - Sub-lease by tenant in violation of lease term - Landlord's suit against tenant and sub-tenant for damages and possession - Judgment of Small Causes. Construction of Section 28 - Implications of sub-tenancy, to be effected.
Whereas the lessee of a flat situated within the city of Bombay had a condition that no sub-letting or re-letting of the premises allotted to the tenant could be allowed without the prior consent of the landlord and the tenant, in violation of this condition, paid the flat at sub-lease, and the landlord filed a suit against him and the sub-tenants, for the use, possession and compensation of the premises in the Small Causes Court, Bombay, and the sublessee claimed that the small causes court had no jurisdiction since he was concerned that the suit was not between the landlord and his tenant nor for rent within the meaning of Section 8 of the Bombay Rents, Regulation of Rates of Hotel Lodging Act, 1947.
(i) that this suit was clearly for possession and that the claim for damages was a mere incidental claim;
(ii) Section 28 of the Act conferred on the Small Causes Court not only to entertain and try any suit or litigation between the landlord and the tenant for recovery of rent or possession, but also "to deal with any claim or question arising out of this Act or any of its provisions" and Section 28 was wide enough to provide that the plaintiff and the sub-leaseholder were sufficient to consider the question raised therein;
(iii) in any case, even though the sub-leaseholder was not a necessary party to the case, he was a competent party and such a joining party cannot alter the nature of the case and cannot diminish the importance of a case between the landlord and the tenant under section 28.

- This is an appeal filed by special leave from a judgment and order dated January 25, 1952 of the Bombay High Court in Civil Revision Application No. 1119 of 1951. It arises out of a case filed in the Small Causes Court at Bombay for the use and occupation of a flat on the third floor of Sunama House, Cumbulla Hill, Bombay, for the purpose of eviction and compensation of Rs. 370 per month with effect from November 1, 1947. The Small Causes court underwent a period of five years and passed a decree directing both the defendants to vacate the flat by March 31, 1951, and pay Rs. 3,311/- 8 per month from November 2, 1947, to July 31, 1948, in respect of the amount of compensation in favour of the first defendant only, and thereafter Rs. 380/- per month from August 1, 1948, in addition to the costs of the case, until possession of the land was obtained. The defendants filed an appeal under Section 29 of that Act. In addition to the various applications before the trial court, the defendants, before the appellate court, moved an additional application, which was not appealed in their written statement, namely, that in so far as it relates to the second defendant, the High Court had no jurisdiction to try the case. The appeal against the judgment of the High Court was dismissed with costs. The second defendant applied to the High Court for an amendment under Section 115 of the Code of Civil Procedure which was dismissed without costs. The Second defendant has now filed an appeal.
The only argument before us is that the Small Causes Court has no jurisdiction to try this case. The relevant parts of Section 8 of the Act are as follows: ---
"Notwithstanding anything contained in any law and notwithstanding anything contained therein, whether by reason of the amount of the claim or otherwise, for the sake of this provision, the suit or proceeding shall not proceed within its jurisdiction,

It was not disputed that the provisions of Part-II of the Act applied to the property. The contention of the appellant is that against this, the case was not a case between the landlord and the tenant and hence:
In so far as it claimed to claim compensation for use and occupancy, it was not a case of recovery of rent and, therefore, Section 8 had no application and the Small Causes Court had no jurisdiction to adjudicate this case. Having determined their rights as owners of Sunamahaa and as trustees of Taraporevala's will, the plaintiffs petitioned that the third floor flat be let to the first defendant on the terms printed on the back of the rent bill form as shown and accepted by the third defendant on September 1, 1942 at a rent of approximately 370 rupees per month. Paragraph 5 of the petition sets out one of those conditions, namely, that the tenant shall not allow the allotment, sub-letting or re-letting of the premises without the prior written consent of the landlords. Paragraph 7 refers to the notice given by the plaintiff to the First defendant on October 17, 1946, to vacate the flat at the end of November 1946, on the ground that two beneficiaries were reasonably and truthfully required under the will to use it. Then, after referring to an unsuccessful attempt on the part of the plaintiff to obtain a certificate under section 9 of the Act, the plaintiff referred to a letter dated November 16, 1947, written by the first defendant to the plaintiffs informing them that from that day he had sub-let the flat to the second defendant. Paragraphs 12 and 13 of the petition state that on December 19, 1947, the second defendant sent a cheque for 370 rupes for the rent for the month of December 1947, but the plaintiffs refused to accept it or to recognise the second defendant as a sub-tenant or otherwise as the lawful occupier. It is further alleged that on January 23, 1948, the plaintiff served a notice on both defendants to vacate their premises by February 29, 1948.
the plaintiffs in paragraphs 1 and 2 constitute grounds for eviction, namely, (1) that the payment of the alleged subpart rent by the first defendant to the second defendant was unjust, unlawful and in violation of the terms of the tenancy and
(2) That the plaintiffs were reasonably and truthfully in need of land suitable for the use and occupation of the two beneficiaries. The plaintiffs pray that both the defendants be directed to vacate the premises and both be directed to pay the plaintiff a compensation of Rs 370 per month from November 1, 1947 for the use of the premises and until the premises are released from occupation. The appellant points out that in the petition the plaintiffs refused to recognise it as a lawful occupier as a sub-tenant or otherwise and treated it as merely a mere intruder without any legal claim to the flat granted on lease and therefore the case in so far as it was one between the plaintiffs and the appellant (the second defendant) cannot be called a case between the landlord and the tenant and in so far As it demanded compensation from him cannot be calleda case for recovery of rent. The last part of the dispute need not hold us back for long, as the case was undoubtedly a case for possession of the flat and the claim for compensation was only an incidental and incidental to the claim for possession. The jurisdiction to suit for possession would not only empower the court to pass a decree for possession but also to direct the payment of the intermediate benefit until the possession was granted. Such a direction for the payment of this intermediate benefit is generally an integral part of the decree for possession. There is only one question for consideration, therefore, whether the case was between the landlords and the tenants.
Defendants (plaintiffs) do not claim, as defined in section 5 (11) of the Act, that the appellant (the second defendant) is a "tenant." On the other hand, the appellant cannot deny that, between the plaintiff and the first defendant, the case is a case between the landlord and the tenant and as such under section 28 of the Act the Small Claims Court is the only court competent to entertain the case. Only between a landlord and a tenant.
The Tenant does not grant the Small Causes Court the jurisdiction to entertain and adjudicate any suit or judgment relating to the recovery of rent or possession of the premises but also to "deal with any claim or question arising out of this Act or any of its provisions." There is no reason to hold that "any claim or question" must be one between the landlord and the tenant. In any case, once there is a case between the landowner and the tenant relating to a recovery of the rent or the possession of the property, the small causes court acquires jurisdiction not only to entertain that case but also to deal with any claims or questions arising outside the law or any part of its provisions" which may properly arise in such a case. The plaintiffs in this case have claimed that the alleged payment of sub-tenants by the first defendant to the second defendant was unlawful both because it was a breach of the tenancy terms and because the first defendant, as a statutory tenant, is not entitled to create a sub-tenancy after the contractual tenancy has been determined and because they question the validity of the second defendant's claim for any protection under the Act. Thus the claim or question of the respective rights of the plaintiff and the second defendant thus raised in the petition certainly arises from the law and the language of section 28 appears to be broad enough to take this into account. In addition to this section, the decree of possession in a case for eviction against a tenant under the common law is binding on that tenant or the person claiming rights through him and can be enforced against a person who was or is not a party to the suit. The non-joining of such a person in the suit does not make the decree any less binding on him. In this sense, therefore, he is not necessarily a necessary party to the eviction case against the tenant. However, it is recognised that such a persons is the appropriate party to the case so that a decision may be taken in his presence as to whether the lease has been properly determined and the plaintiff who owns the land is entitled to regain possession of the place so that he can see that there is no collusion between the landlords and the tenants.

Such a person may be added as a party to the case from the beginning of the case or at a later stage of the case if the court deems it appropriate to do so. The joining of such a competent party cannot change the character of the case and cannot make the case less important between the landlord and the tenant or take it out of section 28 of the Act. Otherwise, the retention of this decision will encourage the diversification of the case so that there will be no end of inconvenience and confusion. In our view, the decision and reasoning of the CJ are sufficiently correct and this appeal must fail. We therefore dismiss the appeal with costs.
The appeal is rejected.
