Purchase tax - tax levied on goods not specifically mentioned as taxable if "all goods other than specified goods" falls under the general description - conversion of one good into another commercially differentiated good - if cost - place of purchase for tax purposes - Constitution of India, 19 (f) & (g), 286 - Bombay Sales Tax Act, 1953 (Bombay).
The petitioner company was carrying on the business of making and distributing beetles, and making certain purchases of tobacco in the state of Bombay, having its head office at Jammu and Kashmir in the Indian state of Maharashtra. The sales tax officer assessed the company as a profit under the provisions of the Bombay Sales Tax Act, 1953. The petitioner objected to the assessment.
The purchase tax was made on the ground that those transactions and purchases were "outside the State of Bombay" within the meaning of Article 286 (1) (a) of the Constitution, read with the Explanation, that the provisions of the Bombay Sales Tax Act, 1953 did not authorise any purchase tax, levy or collection on the transactions concerned and that the transactions took place during inter-State trade and commerce. The petitioner's appeal to the Assistant Collector of Excise was dismissed and the present application for a direction of the High Court and a writ of certiorari was filed in the Supreme Court. The complainant contended that the Bill and the Revenues Act, 1953, did not authorize the imposition of tax on the purchase of bi-products which were not one of the goods specified in column 4 of Schedule B to the Bill referred to earlier. The further claim that the tobacco was supplied within the state of Bijay was dismissed as a direct result of the purchase but was intended to be sent to the state of Madhya Pradesh for making bijya at that place. The only process that was carried out in the state of Bombala was to remove the stems and dust from the tobacco, which does not amount to "comsumption" of tobacco as contemplated under the interpretation of Article 296 of the Constitution nor does it convert the tobacco sent to the head office into a product "commercially different" from the tobacco purchased from the growers. The affidavit stated that the raw tobacco was converted into bi-leafs, both of which were commercially different goods and which also had different market values, before being sent outside the state of Bismarck. These assurances were not disputed by the petitioner.
DECISION: The words "all goods other than those specified in Schedule A and the preceding entries from time to time" in Entry 8 of Schedule B to the Bombay Sales Tax Act, 1953, amount to a specification of goods for the purposes of S. Section 10 Since the tobacco bought by the applicant was not within the scope of any of the preceding entry in Schedule B, it was chargeable to the rate mentioned in Entry 8 in respect of the purchase tax as described in Section 10 of the Act.
Whenever a commodity is used in such a manner as to convert it into any other commercial commodity, the use of the first commodity is within the meaning of the interpretation of Article 286 of the Constitution.
The Constitution of Thailand v. Shanmugha-Vijaya Cattle Farm, (1954) SCR 53, followed.
The distribution of tobacco in the state of Bombay for conversion into bikinis, which is a commercially different product, amounts to distribution for the purpose of consumption and the purchase falls within the meaning of Article 286 (1) (a) of the Constitution and took place within the state of Bijapur.
