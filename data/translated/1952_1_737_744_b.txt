Gurbachan Singh
Vs
The Bombay State and other
[Chief Justice, Patanjali Shastri, Justices, Meher Chand Mahajan, Mukerjea, Das and Chandrashekhar Iyer]
City of Bombay Police Act, 1902, Section 27 (1) - Constitution of India, Article 19 (1) (d), 19 (5) - Whether the provisions relating to exclusion violate the fundamental right of freedom of movement - Justification - Exclusion order - Placement of residence outside Bombay State for legality - Legality.
Section 27 (1) of the City of Bombay Police Act, 1902, does not violate the provisions of Article 19 of the Constitution, since it was enacted in the interest of the general public and this sub-section is applicable in consideration of the class of cases and an externment order was issued under it for the purpose of averting danger, the restrictions that are imposed on the fundamental right of a citizen to free movement which are guaranteed by Article 19 (1) (d) of the Constitution, are reasonable and fall within the ambit of Article 19 (5).
Whether restrictions imposed by a legislative enactment on a citizen's fundamental right given in Article 19 (1) (d) of the Constitution are reasonable or not reasonable within the meaning of proviso (5) of this Article depends as much on the procedural part of the law as on the particular part thereof, and in each instance the court has to see under what circumstances and in what manner the restrictions have been imposed.
Two types of externment orders are provided by sub-section (1) of section 27 of the City of Bombay Police Act, 1902, where the externment is directed from Greater Bombay and the other where the outsider removes himself from the State of Bombay.. In the case of the first class, the externment order must specify the place where the outsider will remove himself and it must indicate the route by which he must reach that place. On the other hand, where the externment was from the State of Bombay, the outsider may stay anywhere of his choice outside the State and no place of residence may be mentioned or required.
Whereas an externment order has been made under Section 27 (1) of the City of Bombay Police Act which directs a person resident in the city of Bombay to remove himself from Greater Bombay and go to his native place at Amritsar and on his application he does not prefer to go to Amritsar and may be allowed to stay at Kalyan, which is outside Greater Bombay, and he was permitted to do so: The order held that, in view of the subsequent request of the outsider which was accepted by the Commissioner of Police, the externment order could be constituted as an externment order from Greater Bombay to Kalyan and was therefore a valid order of externment.
Original Judgment: Petition No. 76 of 1952.
The application under Article 32 of the Constitution of India is for a writ in the nature of mandamus.

Judgment of the Court of Appeal, 7 May 1952
Justice Mukerjea - This is a petition under Article 32 of the Constitution, presented by one Gurbachan Singh praying for a writ in the nature of mandamus, which imposes an externment order on the respondents as well as their subordinates and successors, the petitioner under Section 27 (1) of the City of Bombay Police Act (1902).
The applicant is an Indian citizen and is reported to be living with his father at a place called "Gogri Niwas," Vincent Road, Dadar, while his father is engaged in the business of electrical goods in the city of Bombay. On July 23, 1951, an order was issued to the applicant which was publicly communicated by the Commissioner of Police, Bombay City, under Section 27 (1) of the Bombay Police Act, directing him to remove himself from Greater Bombay and move to his native town of Amritsar in East Punjab. The order specified that the applicant must comply with its instructions within two days from the date of doing so and that he must travel by rail to Amritsar. On July 25, 1951, the applicant filed a petition with the Commissioner of Police and prayed for an extension of time to remove himself from Greater Bombay, and on the basis of this petition, the Commissioner of Police granted him time until July 30 of the following year. On July 30, 1951, the applicant himself wrote a letter to the Commissioner of Police stating that he did not wish to go to Amritsar and prayed that he may be allowed to stay on welfare outside Greater Bombay but within the Bombay State, and that he be given a railway ticket from Dadar to that place. It appears that on the basis of this letter, the police took the applicant to welfare on the evening of July 30, 1951, and left him there. Following this, the applicant initiated proceedings first before the criminal bench of the Court of Appeal under Letters Patent on the original side of the Bombay High Court and then under Articles 226 and 228 of the Constitution, alleging the above-mentioned exclusion order and petitioning this Court for a writ of certiorari to quash it. Both these petitions were dismissed, and the applicant filed before this Court under Article 32 of the Constitution.
It has come on the allegation that her fundamental rights under proviso (d) and (e) of Article 19 (1) of the Constitution have been violated by the externment order.
Mr. Umrigar has argued before us, appearing in support of the petition, firstly that the order of externment is wholly void because it is not in consonance with the provisions of Section 27 (1) of the City of Bombay Police Act. His second argument is that the provisions of Section 27 of the City of Bombay Police Act being in conflict with the Fundamental Rights enshrined in proviso (d) and (e) of Article 19 (1) of the Constitution, stand quashed under Article 13 (1) of the Constitution. The last argument though somewhat flimsily pleaded, that the provision in Section 27 (1) referred to above was discriminatory in its nature and was an offence against Article 14 of the Constitution.
With regard to the first point, it is not disputed on the part of the respondents that the order of externment, as passed by the Commissioner of Police on 23-7-1951, is not in strict conformity with the provisions of Section 27 (1) of the City of Bombay Police Act. In the order, the applicant was directed to remove himself from Greater Bombay but at the same time he has mentioned Amritsar as the place where he had to go. Section 27 (1) of the City of Bombay Police Act provides as follows: -
"Whenever it appears to the Commissioner of Police,
(a) the movement or activity of any person in Greater Bombay, is apprehended or calculated to cause fear, danger or damage to person or property, or there are reasonable grounds to believe that this person is or is about to be engaged in committing an offence involving force or violence, or an offence punishable under Chapter XII, XVI or XVII of the Indian Penal Code, or at the instigation of any such offence, and when
Witnesses, in the opinion of the Commissioner, are not willing to come forward to testify in public against such person because of apprehension as to the safety of their persons or property;
(b)... the Commissioner of Police, by an order in writing thereon has duly provided... such person... may be directed to remove himself or herself to a place outside the State or within the State and by such route and within such period as the Commissioner of Police shall determine and shall not enter the State or be Greater Bombay. "
It is clear from this provision that there are two kinds of externment orders as invented by this sub-section; one, where the extern is directed from Greater Bombay and the other where the extern is required to remove himself from the State of Bombay. Within the first class the order has specified the place thereof, where the extern is required to remove himself and it must be indicated that by the way he is required to reach that place. On the other hand, when the extern is from the State of Bombay, the extern may reside outside the State at any place of his choice and no place of residence may be mentioned or required.
The externment order in the case before us began with a direction to the petitioners to remove themselves only from Greater Bombay. It was'mandatory for the authorities in such cases to specify the place where the outsider was to reside. In fact a place, namely Amritsar, was specified in the order, but since it was not within the State of Bombay, it was beyond the jurisdiction of the Commissioner of Police to name this place. The petitioner is argued, without some coercion, to have violated the order if it was wrong to specify the place within the State where the petitioner was to reside. On the other hand the entire order is intended to indicate
It may be noted that the intention of the Commissioner of Police was to expel the petitioner from outside the State of Bombay and this is evident from the fact that he was directed to go to Amritsar which is situated in another State. It is undoubtedly true that the Commissioner of Police, Bombay, had no authority to designate any place outside the State as the place of residence of outsiders and that direction was void; but that direction certainly has a bearing on the question of the construction of the order, as it indicates that the real purpose of the order was to direct the outsider to remove himself not only from Greater Bombay but also from the State of Bombay. If this was the intention, then no place of residence was required. However, we need no further ado on this aspect of the matter, for we are of the view that whatever irregularities there may have been in the original order, the subsequent conduct of the petitioner which had the approval of the Commissioner of Police would have removed the defects. As stated earlier, on July 30, 1951, the petitioner himself wrote a letter to the Commissioner of Police seeking permission to stay in Kalyan which falls within the State of Bombay. His request was accepted and the police took him to Kalyan on the evening of the 30th. We are of the view that, in the circumstances, the order made on July 23, 1951, may be construed as an order of expulsion from Greater Bombay and though there was a mistake with regard to the place where the outsider was ordered to remove himself, the mistake was rectified by the applicant by choosing Kalyan as the place of residence and that choice has been accepted and implemented by the Police Department. We do not think that there is any material in reality on the first point raised by Umrigar Sahib in this situation.
The second point raised by the learned counsel raises the question whether Section 27 (1) of the City of Bombay Police Act imposes restrictions on the fundamental right of a citizen, which is guaranteed under Article 19 (1) (d) of the Constitution and being in conflict with this fundamental right it is null and void under Article 13 (1) of the Constitution. There is no doubt that the provisions of Section 27 (1) of the Bombay Act are made in the interest of the general public which is dangerous and
The question, therefore, is whether the restrictions imposed by this Act on a citizen's right to free movement fall within the ambit of Article 19 (5) of the Constitution; or in other words whether the restrictions are reasonable; it is quite true that the determination of the question as to whether the restrictions imposed by enactment of a law on the fundamental rights of a citizen referred to in Article 19 (1) (d) of the Constitution are reasonable or not within the meaning of Clause (5) will depend as much on the procedural part of the Act as on the substantive part thereof; the court will have to see in each case the circumstances and the manner in which the restrictions are imposed. The maximum period of an exclusion order made under Section 27 (1) of the Bombay Act is two years and the Commissioner of Police can always permit the outsider to enter the prohibited area before the expiry of that period. Considering the class of cases to which this sub-section applies and the fact that an exclusion order under it is intended to be avoided, it is difficult to say that this provision is unreasonable. The Commissioner of Police may revoke an exclusion order in an appropriate case at any moment as he thinks fit, if in his opinion, the outsider's return to the area from which he was removed ceases to present any danger to the community. As to the procedure to be followed in such cases, Section 27 (4) of the Act provides that before passing an order of expulsion against any person, the Commissioner of Police or any officer authorised by him shall inform such person, in writing, of the general nature of the material allegations against him and give him a reasonable opportunity to explain these allegations. He is permitted to appear through an advocate or attorney and file a written statement for the purpose of clarifying his character and examining the witnesses. The only thing Mr. Umrigar has attempted to do about the propriety of this procedure is to hold that a suspect is not permitted to cross-examine witnesses who have testified against him and on whose evidence the trial was commenced.
In our opinion this in itself would not make the procedure unreasonable considering the admitted intention of the legislature in framing the law. The law is certainly an extraordinary one and it has been framed only for those exceptional cases where a witness is not willing to testify against some bad character in public for fear of violence to their person or property whose presence in a particular area is a threat to the safety of the public residing there. This purpose would be completely defeated if the right to confront or cross-examine these witnesses is given. The power to initiate proceedings under this Act has been vested in a high and responsible officer and he is expected to act with caution and impartiality while discharging his duties under the law. This contention of Mr. Umrigar, therefore, must fail.
The last point made by Mr. Umrigar does not seem to make sense to us. It is true that a procedure different from what is laid down in the ordinary law is provided for certain classes of persons against whom action can be taken under Section 27 (1) of the City of Bombay Police Act, but the discrimination if any is based on a reasonable classification which is within the competence of the legislature to make. In view of the purpose for which the law is intended and the principles underlying it, a departure from the ordinary procedure may certainly be justified as the best means of giving effect to the intention of the legislature. In our opinion, therefore, there is no substance in the earnings and the same shall stand dismissed.
The appeal was rejected.
