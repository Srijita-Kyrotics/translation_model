Justice Mukerjea - This appeal, which has come before us on the basis of a certificate granted by the Madras High Court under Articles 134 (1) (c) and 132 (1) of the Constitution, it was admitted in Criminal Appeal No. 129 of 1952 directed against the judgment of the Appeal of the Division Bench of the Madras High Court, the learned Judges confirmed an order dated February 25, 1952, of the Seventh Presidency Magistrate, Madras, by which the General Sales Tax Act of Madras held the appellant guilty of an offence punishable under Section 15 and sentenced him to pay a fine of Rs. 1, 000,; and failing which he shall suffer imprisonment for a term of 3 months.
The appellant is a partner in a firm of merchants called "Indo-Malayan Trading Company," having its head office in the city of Madras and carrying on the business of selling and buying groundnut oil, sago and groceries. For the period - April 1, 1947, to December 31, 1947 - the company was assessed a sales tax under the Madras Act IX of 1939 on the amount of Rs 37,771 and certain gross 37,75,257 rupees brought in and for failure to pay the same under the provisions of Section 15 of the Act against him resulting in his conviction as aforesaid. The course of business, which is generally followed by the company and which was actually followed during the period of assessment,. In the following manner: The company receives orders from merchants of Calcutta for the supply of certain articles to its Madras office. These articles are purchased from the local market and they are sent to Calcutta by rail or steamer. The bills of lading and receipts of the railway are taken in the name of the seller company and also in the insurance policies and they are sent to the bankers of the company at Calcutta who supply the same to the consignees for payment of prices and other charges. The only thing that needs to be considered is whether, in the circumstances, the sales transactions were liable to be taxed under the general sales tax laws of Madras?
Both parties before the High Court appear to have taken the position that on the basis of the facts set out above, which were not disputed by either party, the sale could be presumed to have taken place within the province of Madras, but the tax could legitimately be levied on them but not otherwise. In determining whether or not the sale took place within the province of Madras, the parties differed as to the test applied. The contention raised for the appellant was that the place of sale in respect of all the transactions was Kolkata, because the property of the goods sold was received by the purchasers of that city. On the other hand the respondent argued that the true test for determining the place of sale was not where the property of the goods sold was received, but where the actual transaction was made. Since the head office of the company was in the city of Madras, its accounts were maintained there, and the goods were delivered to the common carrier of that city, according to the respondent, the sale must be deemed to have taken place in Madras even though the property of the goods sold went out of the province.
The High Court accepted this argument of the defendant's statement. According to the learned Judges, the word "sale" has both legal and popular connotations. In the legal sense, it implies the importation of the property of the goods, and in this sense the word is used in the law of sale of goods. In the popular sense, however, it refers to the transaction itself which results in the sale of the property of the goods. Since the object of the legislature in the sales tax law is to impose a tax on the occasion of the sale, it is not necessary that the sale be completed outside the province. In the place where the property passes, it is said, the concern of the taxing authority is not involved, and in this context the popular meaning of the word should be more appropriate and accepted. Further contention raised on the part of the appellant, that if this view is adopted, the sales tax must be regarded as extra-territorial in its operation and as transcending the provincial legislature, was struck down by the High Court on the authority of the well-known decision of the Judicial Committee in Wallace Brothers, and Company v. Commissioner of Income Tax, Bombay (1).
The correctness of this decision which has been challenged before us, and the contentions raised by Mr. Somaiya, who appeared in support of the appeal, are of a two-faced character: the learned counsel has first argued that the provincial law is operative under the Government of India Act, 1935, was constitutionally incapable of enacting a law in this regard which is capable of dealing with sales transactions concluded outside the province as per the interpretation given by the High Court. The other contention is that the High Court ought to have approved the imposition of sales tax in respect of transactions of sales where the property of the goods sold passes outside the province, for the proper construction of the relevant provisions of the Madras Sales Tax Act.
The first contention does not appear to us to be supportable.Government of India Act 1935 Section 100 (3)

"Subject to the two preceding sub-clauses, the Provincial Legislature has the power, and the Federal Legislature does not have, to make laws for a province or any part thereof with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List II in the Second Schedule."
The entry in the Provincial List which is relevant to our purposes is Entry No. 48 and speaks of taxes on the sale and advertisement of goods. The entry does not propose that a law imposing a tax on the sale of goods may be made in respect of sales made within the boundaries of the province; and that section 100 (3) provides that a law may be passed by a provincial legislature for the purposes of that province. It acknowledges without dispute that a provincial legislature may not pass a tax code which would be binding in any other part of India outside the limits of the province, but that it would be sufficiently competent to enact a law to impose a tax on transactions concluded outside the province., provided that such transactions and taxing were substantial and had a real territorial relationship within the province. This principle, based on the decision of the Judicial Committee in Wallace Brothers et al. and Company v. Commissioner of Income Tax, Bombay (1) has been held by this Court in its recent decision to be applicable to the Sales Tax Act, in the Bombay Sales Tax Act case (2) and its availability beyond question. In fact, the practice of law relating to sales tax legislation adopted by provincial legislatures before the Constitution came into force was to authorise the imposition of a tax on sales and purchases which were in some way related to the tax-paying province within the province on account of some element of the transaction or

The reason for the production or the location of the goods at the time when the transaction has taken place within this. If the basis adopted for taxation in the Madras Sales Tax Act is the place of business or the location of the goods sold within the Province of Madras, it would undoubtedly be a valid law to which there can be no objection on constitutional grounds. The debate is therefore narrowed down to the brief point of what exactly has been adopted as the basis for the levy of sales tax by the Madras Legislature. This leads us to the question of the interpretation of the law which is involved in the second point raised by Mr. Somaiya.
It is a settled rule of construction to ascertain the intention to make laws, to bind together all the constituent parts of the law, and to consider each word, phrase, or sentence in the light of the general object and purpose of the law. The title of the Madras Sales Tax Act describes it as an Act, the object of which is to levy a general tax on the sale of goods in the Province of Madras, and the same words are repeated in the preamble which follow. The title and preamble, whatever their value may be as aids to the construction of a statute, undoubtedly throw light on the intention and plan of the legislature, and indicate the scope and purpose of the law. The title and preamble of the Madras Sales Tax Act clearly show that its object is to impose a tax on sales made within the province, although these words do not mean that the property of the goods sold must go within the province. The expression "sale of goods" is a composite expression consisting of various elements or elements. There are, thus, elements of bargaining or contracts of sale, of promises or undertakings to pay the price, of the supply of goods, and of passing the actual title, and each of them is essential to the transaction of sale even though the sale is not complete or concluded until the purchaser owns the property. The question is which element or material has been received.
The Madras Legislature constitutes a sale in the Province on which it is the object of law to levy a tax. Section 2 (h) defines "sale," and it is defined as money, the transfer of every article by one person to another in the course of trade or business for cash or for delayed payment or other valuable consideration, but not including mortgage, hypothecation, charge or pledge. "
No doubt this definition stresses the substance of the transfer of property in the sale and no other. The language gives no indication of the popular meaning of sale where, in the opinion of the High Court, the term was used. It is to be noted that there was no provision by way of interpretation of this definition, in operation, to indicate in what cases the sale would be deemed to have taken place within the Province of Madras, even though the sale of the property in the goods went beyond the boundaries of the Province. Such interpretations were added by the Madras Act XXV of 1947 and one of these interpretations, namely Explanation 2, provides as follows:
"Notwithstanding anything to the contrary contained in the Sale of Indian Goods Act, 1930, for the purposes of this Act the sale or purchase of any goods shall be deemed to have taken place in this Province, wherever the contract of sale or purchase may have been made -
(a) if the goods were actually in this province, at the time when the contract of sale or purchase was entered into, or
(b) if the contract was for the sale or purchase of future goods by description, then, if the goods are actually produced in this province at any time after the contract of sale or purchase has been made. "
It will be clear from this that these transactions were not considered by the Legislature to constitute sales within the Province of Madras under the definition, but resort to a legal conflict which by them
It was declared, notwithstanding any provision contrary to the Sale of Goods Act, it was presumed, otherwise, to apply. The Explanation further shows that in defining "sale" in section 2 (h), the Legislature had in mind a sale in the Province of Madras, and as these words appear in the title and preamble to the Act, it was not considered necessary to repeat them in the definition or charging section. Section 3 is the charging section of the Act and it provides for the imposition of a tax on the total turnover of a dealer for a particular year. A "turnover" is defined as the aggregate amount for which goods are bought or sold. The charging section is intended to impose a tax on the sale of goods and the tax is levied on the sale of goods in the Province of Madras as defined in section 2 (h) of the Act read in the light of the title and preamble to the Act.
In our opinion, the transaction is not entered into merely because the contract for sale was entered into within the Province of Madras, which was admittedly concluded within the other Province, where the property of the goods was received, a sale within the Province of Madras is in accordance with the provisions of the Madras Sales Tax Act and no tax can be imposed on such transaction under the provisions of the Act. A contract of sale becomes a sale only under the Sales of Goods Act when the property of the goods is transferred to the purchaser under the terms of the contract. The presence of the goods within the Province at the time of the contract would undoubtedly make the sale, if subsequently completed, a sale within the Province by reason of the interpretation added by Act XXV of 1947; but as this interpretation was not in force during the relevant period with which we are concerned, in our opinion, the assessment of sales tax on the transaction during this period is unlawful and not warranted by the provisions of the Act. It is necessary to point out in this connection that no tax was sought to be imposed on a similar transaction of the appellant by the taxing authority in any case except during the period in question. The previous year, although the Act came into force in early 1939. It is also not disputed that the company is paying sales tax on its transactions with the merchants of Calcutta 1947
From the coming into force of the interpretation added by Law XXV. of the year, in our opinion, the appeal should be allowed and the conviction and sentence handed down by the court below should be set aside. The fine and sales tax, if actually paid, should be refunded to the appellant.
The appeal has been allowed.
