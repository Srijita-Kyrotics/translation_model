Justice Mukerjee - This appeal, which has come before us on the basis of a certificate granted by the Maharashtra High Court under Articles 134 (1) (c) and 132 (1) of the Constitution, was admitted in Criminal Appeal No. 129 of 1952, directed against the judgment of the Appeal Board of the High Court, by which the learned judges confirmed an order dated February 25, 1952, of the Seventh Presidency Magistrate, in which the General Sales Tax Act of 1935 held the appellant guilty of an offence punishable under Section 15 and sentenced him to pay a fine of Rs. 1,000; and failing which he shall suffer imprisonment for a term of three months.
The appellant is a partner in a firm of merchants called "Indo-Malayan Trading Company," having its head office in the city of Madras and carrying on the business of selling and buying groundnut oil, and soybeans and groceries. For the period - from April 1, 1947, to December 31, 1947 - the company was assessed a sales tax under the Madras Act IX of 1939 on the amount of Rs. 37,771 and certain gross income of Rs. 3,751,257 and for failure to pay the same under the provisions of Section 15 of the Act against him resulting in his conviction as aforesaid. The course of business, which is generally followed by the company and which was actually followed during the period of assessment,. In the following manner: The company receives orders from merchants in Calcutta for the supply of certain articles to its Mumbai office. These articles are purchased from the local market and sent to Calcuttar by train or ship. The bills of lading and receipts of the railway are taken in the name of the company and also in the insurance policies and they are sent to the bankers of the company at Calcuttar who supply the same to the customers for payment of prices and other charges. The only thing that needs to be considered is whether, in the circumstances, the sales transactions were liable to be taxed under the general sales tax laws of Malaysia?
Both parties before the High Court appeared to have taken the position that on the basis of the facts set out above, which were not disputed by either party, the sale could be presumed to have taken place within the Province of Madras, but the tax could be genuinely levied on them but not otherwise. In determining whether or not the sale took place within the Kingdom of Mandisa, the parties differed as to the test applied. The contention raised in favour of the appellant was that the place of sale in respect of all transactions was in Kolkata, as the property of the goods sold had been received by the purchasers of that city. On the other hand, the respondent argued that the true test for determining the place of sales was not where the property of goods sold was received, but where the actual transaction was made. Since the head office of the company was in the city of Mandras, its accounts were maintained there and the goods were delivered to the common carrier of that city, according to the respondent, the sale must be regarded as having taken place in Mandras even though the property of those goods had passed outside the province.
The High Court accepted this argument of the defendant's statement. According to the learned judges, the word "sale" has both legal and popular connotations. In the legal sense, it implies the importation of the property of the goods, and in this sense the word is used in the law of sale of goods. In the popular sense, however, it refers to the transaction itself which results in the sale of the property to the goods. Since the object of the legislature in the sales tax law is to impose a tax on the occasion of the sale, it is not necessary that the sale be completed outside the province. In the place where the property passes, it is said, the concern of the taxing authority is not involved, and, in this context, the popular meaning of the word should be more appropriate and accepted. Further contention raised on the part of the appellant, that if this view is accepted, the sales tax must be regarded as extra-territorial in its operation and as transcending the provincial legislature, was struck down by the High Court on the authority of the well-known decision of the Judical Committee in Wallace, v. Commissioner of Income Tax, Bombay (1).
The correctness of this decision which has been challenged before us, and the contentions raised by Mr. Somaiya, who appeared in support of the appeal, are of a two-faced character: the learned counsel has first argued that the provincial law is operative under the Government of India Act, 1935, which was constitutionally incapable of enacting a law in this regard which is capable of dealing with sales transactions concluded outside the province as per the interpretation given by the High Court. The other contention is that the High Court ought to have approved the imposition of sales tax in respect of transactions of sales where the property of the goods sold passes outside the province, for the proper construction of the relevant provisions of the Malaysian Sales Tax Act.
The first contention does not appear to us to be supportable. Government of India Act 1935, Section 100(3).

on which Mr. Somaiya based his argument and which is in conformity with Article 246(3) of the Constitution:
"Subject to the two preceding sub-clauses, the Provincial Legislature has the power, and the Federal Legislature does not have, to make laws for a province or any part of it with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List II in the Second Schedule."
The entry in the Provincial List which is relevant to our purposes is Entry No. 48 and speaks of taxes on the sale and advertisement of goods. The entry does not propose that a law imposing a tax on the sale of goods may be made in respect of sales made within the boundaries of the Province; and that section 100(3) provides that a Law may be passed by a Provincial legislature for the purposes of that Province. It acknowledges without doubt that a provincial legislature may not pass a tax code which would be binding in any other part of India outside the limits of the Province, but that it would be sufficiently competent to enact a law to impose a tax upon transactions concluded outside the Province, provided that such transactions and taxing were substantial and had a real territorial relationship within the Province. This principle, based on the decision of the Judical Committee in Wallace v. Board of Inquiry, 2 B.C., has been held by this Court in its recent decision to be applicable to the Sales Tax Act, in the Bombay Sales Tax Act (2) and its availability is beyond question. In fact, the practice of law relating to sales tax legislation adopted by provincial legislatures before the Constitution came into force was to authorise the imposition of tax on sales and purchases which were in some way related to the tax-paying province within the Province on account of some element of the transaction or

The reason for the production of the goods or the location of the goods at the time when the transaction has taken place within this. If the basis for taxation in the Malaysian Sales Tax Act is the place of business or the locations of the goods sold within the Province of Malaysia, it would undoubtedly be a valid law to which there can be no objection on constitutional grounds. The debate is therefore narrowed down to the brief point of what exactly has been accepted as the basis for the levy of sales tax by the Malaysian Legislature. This leads us to the question of the interpretation of the law which is involved in the second point raised by Mr. Somaiya.
It is a settled rule of construction to ascertain the intention to make laws, to bind together all the constituent parts of the law, and to consider each word, phrase, or sentence in the light of the general object and purpose of the law. The title of the Madras Sales Tax Act describes it as an Act, the object of which is to levy a general tax on the sale of goods in the Province of Madras, and the same words are repeated in the preamble which follows. The title and preamble of the Ramasasi Sales Tax Act clearly show that its object is to impose a tax on sales made within the province, although these words do not mean that the property of the goods sold must go within the province. The expression "sale of goods" is a composite expression consisting of various elements or elements. There are, thus, elements of bargaining or contracts of sale, of promises or undertakings to pay the price, of the supply of goods, and of passing the actual value, and each of them is essential to the transaction of sale even though the sale is not complete or concluded until the purchaser owns the property. The question is whether the element or material has been received.
The Malaysian Legislature constitutes a sale in the Province on which it is the object of law to levy a tax. Section 2 (h) defines "sale," and it is defined as the transfer of every article by one person to another in the course of trade or business for cash or for delayed payment or other valuable consideration, but not including mortgage, hypothecation, charge or promise. "
No doubt this definition stresses the substantial transfer of property in the sale and no other. The language gives no indication of the popular meaning of the term, in the opinion of the High Court, where the term was used. It is to be noted that there was no provision by way of interpretation of this definition, in operation, to indicate in what cases the sale would be deemed to have taken place within the Province of Mandisa, even though the sale of the property in the goods went beyond the boundaries of the Province. Such interpretations were added by the Malaysian Act XXV of 1947 and one of these interpretations, namely Explanation 2, provides as follows.
"Notwithstanding anything to the contrary contained in the Sale of Indian Goods Act, 1930, for the purposes of this Act the sale or purchase of any goods shall be deemed to have taken place in this Province, wherever the contract of sale or purchase may have been made-
(a) If the goods were actually in this province, at the time when the contract of sale or purchase was entered into, or
(b) if the contract was for the sale or purchase of future goods by description, then, if the goods are actually produced in this province at any time after the contract of sale or purchase has been made. "
It will be clear from this that these transactions were not considered by the Legislature to constitute sales within the Province of Malaysia under the definition, but were instead a legal challenge to which they were subject.
It was declared, notwithstanding any provision contrary to the Sale of Goods Act, that it was, otherwise, to apply. The Explanation further shows that in defining "sale" in section 2 (h), the Legislature had in mind a sale in the Province of Mandis, and as these words appear in the title and preamble to the Act, it was not considered necessary to repeat them in the definition or charging section. Section 3 is the charging section of the Act and it provides for the imposition of a tax on the total turnover of a dealer for a particular year. A "turnover" is defined as the amount of money that is bought or sold. The charging section is intended to impose a tax upon the sale of goods and the tax is levied on the sale of good in the Province Of Mandis as defined in section 2 h) of the Act read in the light of the title and the preamble of the Act.
In our opinion, the transaction is not entered into merely because the contract for sale was entered into within the Province of Mandisa, which was admittedly concluded within the other Province, where the property of the goods was received, a sale within the Provincial of Mandsa is in accordance with the provisions of the Madras Sales Tax Act and no tax can be imposed on such a transaction under the provisions of this Act. A contract of sale becomes a sale only under the terms of the Contract for the Sale of Goods. The presence of the goods within the Province at the time of the contract would undoubtedly make the sale, if subsequently completed, within the Province by reason of the interpretation added by Act XXV of 1947; but as this interpretation was not in force during the relevant period with which we are concerned, in our opinion, that the assessment of sales tax on the transaction during this period is unlawful and not justified by the passage of the Act. It is necessary to point out in this connection that no tax was sought to be imposed upon a similar transaction of the appellant by the taxing authority in any case except during the period in question. The previous year, although the Act came into force in early 1939. It is also not disputed that the company is paying sales tax on its transactions with the merchants of Calcutta in 1947.
From the coming into force of the interpretation added by Law XXV. of the year, in our opinion, the appeal should be allowed and the conviction and sentence handed down by the court below should be set aside. The fine and sales tax, if actually paid, should be refunded to the appellant.
The appeal has been allowed.
