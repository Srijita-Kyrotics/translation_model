Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Act, 1946, Section 4 - Extension of the Act to March 31, 1951, Resolution of the Constituent Assembly - Justification by - Powers of the Constituent Assembly India (Central Government and Legislature) Act, 1946, Section 4, 4-A - India (Provisional Constitution) Order, 1947 - Constitution of India, 1950, Articles 372, 379 (1), 394.
The Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Act, 1946, a temporary law extended from time to time after the first expiration date for a period of one year, to March 31, 1951, by a resolution passed by the Constituent Assembly (Legislative) at a meeting held on March 31, 1950, to December 20, 1949. The appellant, who was convicted of an offence committed under the Act on October 24, 1950, claimed that the Constituent Assembly had no power to extend the Act in terms of the provisions of Article 379 (1) of the Constitution, and at any rate had no power to extend the Act beyond January 26, 1950;
The Order, which, even assuming under Article 379 (1) that the Provisional Parliament was intended to function from November 26, 1949, and not from January 26, 1950, as the Constituent Assembly continued till January 26, 1950, and was vested with powers as a body under the India (Central Government and Legislature) Act, 1946, of the British Parliament, in the same manner as the India (Provisional Constitution) Order, 1947, could have been validly exercised on December 20, 1949, and thus given effect when the ratification was passed on that date. The Provisional Parliament was not a body authorised to exercise the special powers of granting extensions mentioned in Section 4 India Act, 1946, which did not give the Provisional Parliament one of the powers conferred by the Constitution, nor could it bring the Provisional Parliament into existence on November 26, 1949, presuming that the case would be considered as "another provision" made by the Constituent Assembly under Section 4 India Act 1946.
It is further held that the proposal to extend the life of the Act beyond January 26, 1950, was not justified, as it came into force immediately and not on April 1, 1950, when the previous extension had expired. Accordingly, the Act with the extended term on the basis of the proposal was an Act in force immediately before the commencement of the Constitution and was therefore preserved in Article 372 (1) and and Explanation III.
Criminal Appeal Judicature: Case No. 275 of 1951. Appeal under Article 132 (1) of the Constitution of India from judgment and order dated April 11, 1951 in Criminal Revision Case No. 1028 of 1950 in the Court of the High Court of Calcutta (Das Gupta and Mukherjee JJ) dated November 23, 1950, in Presidency Magistrate, Eighth Court, Calcutta, পি.আর of 1950. এন.সি.Chakraborty for the appellant.

এম.সি.Setalvad, Attorney-General for India (P. A. Mehta, with him), for the intervener.
Judgment of the Court was delivered on December 5, 1952.
Judge Bose This is an appeal under Article 132 (1) of the Constitution.The Calcutta High Court has allowed the appeal.
The appellant was convicted under Section 7 (1) A of the Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Act, 1946, for the offence committed on October 24, 1950. The conviction was on two counts: (1) above the regulated rate for the sale of cloth and (2) for non-issuance of cash memo. The sentence was three months rigorous imprisonment with a fine of Rs 200 and a further three months in default. The trial was held before the Eighth Presidency Magistrate, Kolkata who adopted a summary procedure.
Revision was appealed to the High Court but was dismissed. Permission was then sought to appeal to this court. It was granted on a ground that had not been taken in revision in the original court or before the High Court, namely that the Essential Supplies Act of 1946 under which the appellant had been convicted was not in force on October 28, 1950, and therefore there could be no conviction under it.
The fairness of this Act was challenged in Jayalal Agarwala v. State (1) but this Court held that the Act was fair up to March 31, 1950, that is, the life of the Act on the date relevant to that case.. It is necessary to explain that the Act is a temporary Act and its life has been extended from time to time for a period of one year beyond the date of its first expiry. The last extension on the date of the previous case was up to March 31, 1950. We are therefore beginning with the position that the Act was a good Act up to this date.
By a resolution dated December 20, 1989 this Act was extended till March 31, 1951. This is the extension which we are concerned about and which is now being objected to, the argument being that there was no legislative body in existence on that date capable of extending the life of the Act for another year.
The newspaper notice setting out the proposal stipulates the following conditions:
(1) [1952] এস.সি.আর.127.
"New Delhi, December 22, 1989.
No এফ.-7 W L (1) 47.-নিম্নলিখিত Resolution which was adopted by the Constituent Assembly (Legislative) at its meeting held on December 20, 1949, is hereby released for general information:
Pursuant to the provisions of section 4 of the India (Central Government and Legislature) Act, 1946, as adapted by the India (Provisional Constitution) Order, 1947, this Assembly hereby approves the extension of the period referred to in sections 2 and 3 of the said Act for a further period of twelve months commencing on the first day of April, 1950. "
It remains to be seen whether the body that passed that resolution has the power to extend the law.
It can be assumed, by virtue of the decision in Jayalal Agarwala v. The State (1), that the Constituent Assembly had the power to make two successive extensions of the Essential Commodities Act every year on February 25, 1948, and again on March 23, 1989. The only question, therefore, is whether any such body retains that power beyond that date.
The said extensions were brought about as follows. The Constituent Assembly was empowered to pass the above resolution by virtue of Section 4A of the India (Central Government and Legislature) Act, 1946. It was an Act of the British Parliament which originally vested the power of ratification in the British Parliament. Extension of time prescribed by Section 4 by resolution. Later, the Indian Independence Act, 1947 was passed by the British Parliament and replaced by an Adaptation Order by the Governor-General to exercise the powers conferred by Sections 9 and 19 of that Act, the term "Dominion Legislature" for the term "House of Parliament"
(1) [1952] SCR 127 A 131.
and thus enabled the Dominion Legislature to exercise the powers of Parliament in this behalf. At the same time, the Governor-General by adaptation introduced in section 4 in the British Act of 1946, the India (Central Government and Legislature) Act, 1946, and conferred on the Constituent Assembly the powers of the Dominion Legislature. The Constituent Assembly was thus empowered by the passing of a resolution to extend the time limit prescribed in section 4, and this had the effect of extending the life of the Essential Supplies Act, 1946, because section 1 (3) of that Act states that it shall cease to have effect after the expiry of the period referred to in section 4 of the India (Central Government and Legislature) Act, 1946.
Now Section 4A provides that the Dominion Legislature shall have powers under British law until other provisions are made by or in accordance with a law made by the Constituent Assembly under sub-section (1) of section 8 of the Indian Independence Act, 1947. "
Turning to sub-section (1) of section 8, we find that the British Parliament has invested the Constituent Assembly with all the powers of the Dominion Legislature "for the purpose of making provisions as to the Constitution of the Dominion."
This power exercised it and formulated the Indian Constitution, but in doing so it decided to bring the Constitution in two instalments and it enacted Article 394 and legislated in it that that Article and some more, including Article 379, should be legislated, coming into force "forthwith" - that is, on November 26, 1949 when the remaining Articles were to come into force on January 26, 1950.
Now Article 379 (1) provides that -
"So long as both Houses of Parliament are duly constituted and not summoned for the first session under the provisions of this Constitution, the body functioning immediately before the commencement of this Constitution as the Constituent Assembly of the Dominion of India shall be temporary. Parliament and shall exercise all the powers and perform all the duties conferred by the provisions of this Constitution on Parliament."
It was argued on behalf of the appellant that by virtue of this Article the Constituent Assembly had disappeared as a legislative body on and after 26 November 1949 and the Provisional Parliament referred to by that Article had taken its place on 20 December 1949, implying the resolution was a resolution of the Constituent Assembly (Legislative) and not of the Provisional Parliament, a proposal of a body which no longer had the power to legislate the laws of the country or pass such a resolution.
On the other hand, the learned Attorney-General argues that the Constituent Assembly continued to function in this manner and retained the right to exercise the dual functions of framing the Constitution and making laws until the midnight of 25 January 1950. In the next second, when a new day ushered in a new era for this country, it ceased to exist as a Constituent Assembly and its place was taken by the Provisional Parliament of India.
We do not need to decide on this, because the Provisional Parliament was in operation on November 26, 1949, and not from January 26, 1950, it is clear that the Constituent Assembly will continue until the "stay" the commencement of the Constitution "which, by Article 398, is dated January 26, 1950. Consequently, the powers conferred on it by English law as a designated body, as adapted by the Governor-General, may be fairly exercised. December 20, 1949, and this was exercised when the resolution of that date was passed. The Provisional Parliament was not the body authorised to exercise the special powers of granting extensions referred to in section 4 of the English Act because it was not one of the powers conferred by this Constitution on Parliament and nothing could bring the Provisional Parliament into existence on the date of November 26, 1949.
(assuming that it would) be deemed to be "other provisions" made by the Constituent Assembly within the meaning of that in section 4 of the English Act. It follows that on the date of the proposal the Constituent Assembly was not deprived of this specially designated power.
The next question is whether the Constituent Assembly had the power to extend the life of this particular law beyond January 26, 1950. The question was thus posed. It was admitted that the Essential Supplies Act was validly extended till March 31, 1950. The resolution which extended its life by another year beyond this was passed on December 20, 1949, but it was argued that it could not be accepted. It is not in force until after the expiry of the previous extended period, i.e., April 1, 1950. But by then the Constitution has come into force and therefore neither the Constituent Assembly nor the Provisional Parliament could extend the temporary period after its expiry on March 31, 1950 because of Article 372, Explanation III. It follows that the Constituent Assembly which intended to effect the extension prematurely could not, in anticipation, do what the Constitution says cannot be done later.
There is nothing in this debate. The resolution of December 20, 1949, comes into force immediately, and its result is to change the date fixed in section 4 of the English law for the expiration of the term mentioned in it from March 31, 1950, to March 31, 1951. Essential ProvisionsI have fixed the date of expiration of its own term as the date of expiration of the term mentioned in section 4 above. Accordingly, it was an Act which was alive just before January 26, 1950, and it was due to expire of its own force which, at that time, was not on March 31, 1950, but up to March 31, 1951, and it was an Act in force immediately after the commencement of the Constitution.
It was in force because of Article 372 (1) and Explanation III, until it expired.
This puts an end to the constitutional issues. We hold that a body existed at all material times to extend the life of the Act until March 31, 1951, and that it was able to extend its life on December 20, 1949. The Act continued to be a living law even after the Constitution was in force on the date of the offence, that is, October 28, 1950.
The Counsel then sought to plead guilty on other grounds but as the appeal application was confined to constitutional matters, he could no longer be permitted to speak. Of course, it was appropriate for him to make a separate application for special leave to appeal on other matters but had he done so it would have followed the usual rules and he would have been obliged to obtain special leave in the normal course. We have therefore considered this part of the arguments as seeking special leave to appeal. We have heard him fully and are of the opinion that these remaining opinions are not those on which special leave to appeal should be granted. We therefore refuse this irregular application for special leave to appeal on its merits.
The appeal filed under Article 132 (1) was also dismissed.
The appeal was rejected.
