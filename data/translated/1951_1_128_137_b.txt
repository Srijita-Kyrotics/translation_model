The assesses were assessed at a higher rate prescribed by the Penal Law as there was no income tax act in New Delhi on August 20, 1948.
And hence no income tax proceedings were pending in Nabha. The petitioner who was an assessee residing in Nirmala and who was assessed under the Pitala Act for a wrong in the nature of 2/3 of the Constitution quashed the assessment of certitude on the ground (i) that he is entitled to the fundamental right of equality before the law and that Article 14 of the Constitution guaranteed by the article denies the equal protection of the law since he was assessed at a higher rate than the way the assessees of Kapurthala were assessed, (ii) that, since the Ordinance enacting the Ptiala Income Tax Act in Nabhaka was enacted on August 20, 1948, it cannot operate retrospectively and authorise the levy of tax on the income accumulated during the year ended on December 12, 1948 and for that reason he was threatened with the violation of the fundamental right guaranteed by Article 31 (1) of the Constitution that no one shall be deprived of his property in accordance with the security of the law:

By Justice Das - This is a petition under Article 32 of the Constitution for a proper order protecting what he claims to be his fundamental right guaranteed by Articles 1 and 3. This is said to be a test case, for his decision, we say, depends on the rights of numerous other persons whose interests are the same as those of the petitioner.
There is no serious dispute as to the material material for the purpose of this petition. They are summarized as follows: On May 5, 1948, the then rulers of the eight Punjab states, including Punjab and Nabha, with the consent and guarantee of the Government of India, entered into an agreement and agreed to unite their territories into a common state called Punjab, with an executive, legislative and judiciary. And the Union of Eastern Punjab States was hereby called the PEPSU. By Article III (6) of the Agreement, the then king of Punjab became the first president of the Raja Rampaukh of the Governing Council and held the office during his lifetime. The following is the text of Article VI of the Agreement: -
"(1) The Ruler of each Contracting States shall, as soon as may be, and in any case not later than August 20, 1948, hand over the administration of his state to the Head of the States, and thereupon,
(a) all rights, authority and jurisdiction belonging to the Ruler or which shall be exercable to the Union in the Government of the Conciliating States concerned or otherwise only by virtue of this Agreement or as otherwise provided by the Constitution;
(b) all the duties and obligations of the Ruler relating to or incidental to the Government of the Contracting States shall vest in the Union and shall be performed by it;
(c) all the assets and liabilities of the Contracting Party shall be the property of the Union,
(d) The military forces of the Contracting States, if any, shall become the political forces of the Union."
To constitute a Constituent Assembly by reference to Article X which shall provide for the framing of a single type of Constitution for the Union within the Constitution of India and the framework thereof. "The Head of State shall have power to make and promulgate ordinances for the peace and good government of the Union or any part of it, until a Constitution made by the Constituent Assembly comes into operation after the consent of the Head of State has been obtained. In this situation, the information leading to the current application must be considered.
The petitioner is a resident of Ateli in the Mohindergarh district which is now in Pepsi but which was earlier a part of Nabha state. The petitioner has been running his business in Ateli for several years in the name of Ramachandra Rai Rampa. He has never paid any income tax as no such tax has been imposed by any law in Nabha State. On October 20, 1949, the petitioner was served a notice under section 22 (2) and 38 of the Punjabi Income Tax Act, 2001, requiring him to file a return for the income tax year 2006 (13-4-1949 to 13-5-1950) relating to his income during the previous year (12-4-1948 to 12-5-1949). The petitioner, on December 4, 1949, filed his return for the year 2006 and was assessed income tax on February 14, 1950. On May 23, 1950, the tax payer received a notice in relation to section 34 of the Income Tax Act asking him to file his return for that year ending on the last day of the tax year, i.e., from 13-4-1948 until 13-5-1949. In this return he had to mention his previous year's income, namely, 2004 (i.e, from 13-5-1947 to 13-3-1948). It appears that the petitioner, along with other assesses of Atele and Kalina, submitted an application to the Income Tax Officer on July 9, 1950, not to proceed with the assessment for the year 2005 but on July 13, 1950, the Income Tax officer assessed him under section 22 (4) and section 34 (4) of the income tax act. With other assessees similar to him, the taxer approached the Commissioner of Income Tax and the Central Board of Revenue, New Delhi, but without any success. No formal appeal under the Punjabi income tax act seems to have been filed by the petitioner against the assessment of his income for the two years 2005 and 2006. On August 10, 1950, the respondent filed his present application before this Court under Article 32 of the Constitution to issue a writ in the nature of a whit of certiorari to quash the assessment and other ancillary remedies of the applicant's income collected in 2004 and 2005. During the pendency of this application the Income Tax authorities issued an order under section 46.
The contention of the petitioner in the first place is that he has been deprived of the fundamental right to equality of law guaranteed to him by Article 7 of the Constitution and deprived of equal protection of law. His allegations have been formulated in paragraphs 10 and 11 of his petition. It is stated that when the people of Kapurthala belonging to PEPSU have been asked to pay income tax for the period prior to August 20, 1948, at the rate prescribed by the earlier Kapurtala Income Tax Act, which was lower than the rate prescribed to pay the Ptiala Income Tax Bill, 2001, the people of Nabha who were not required to pay any income tax before August 20, 1949, have been made liable to pay the higher rate in Ptiala and such discrimination constitutes an offence against the provisions of Article 14. This allegation is rebutted by paragraph 10 of the affidavit of Sardar Gurbax Singh, Inspector of Income Tax (Income Tax), New Delhi, who was formerly Commissioner, Income Tax, Punjab and PEPSU, which has been filed in opposition to the present petition. Therein it is stated that for the assessment of 2005, the assesses whose cases were pending in Kaurthala were assessed on August 20, 1948 , at the same rate prescribed under the Kaurtala income tax act but the provisions of the Piala income tax law for the assessment year 2006 and the rates prescribed thereunder were applied uniformly to all areas of PEPSU including Kaurthalha. This allegance which has not been rebutTED in the reply to the petitioner must be accepted as correct. The assessment of assesses for the year 2005 at the old Piala rate was clearly made under the provisions of Section 3 of Ordinance No. 1 of 2005, which was reproduced in terms of Section 3 (1) of Ordinance no. XVI of 2006 and both required that all pending proceedings be completed in accordance with the law applicable to all the proceedings when they were initiated. There was no assessment case pending against any of the NAVA assesses on August 8, 1948, as there was no Income Tax Act in NaVA before that date and, therefore, there could not have been any occasion to complete any pending proceedings against any of such assesses.
Appearing in support of the petition, Dr. Tekchand subsequently claimed that the administration of Nabha state was taken over by the Rampantha Rao only on August 20, 1948 and the Ptiala Act was brought along with the Pitala Income Tax Act, 2001 on August 20, 1949 and since then, the assessment of the tax on the income of the petitioner which was collected prior to August 20, 1948, is totally illegal and not authorised by the said Ordinance and the state so as to illegally assess the attack on the fundamental right of the complainant for property guaranteed by Article 31 (1) of the Constitution was terrifying.
Article 31 (1) provides as follows:
"(1) No person may be deprived of his property except by authority of law."
It will be noted that Section (1) reproduces, without the words "in British India," subsection (1) of Part 299 of the Government of India Act, 1935. The reference is made to Article 265 which deals with Chapter XII, Part II, "Money." That article provides that no tax may be levied or collected except by authority of law. There was no similar provision in a similar chapter of the Government Of India Act, 1936. If the amount of tax collected amounts to deprivation of property within the meaning of Article 31 (1), then again there is no point in making a separate provision in Article 265. Therefore, Section (1) of Article 31 must be treated as relating to depriving property rather than imposition or collection of tax, otherwise Article 265 becomes wholly redundant. The power to tax in the United States is held to be distinct from the exercise of police powers or eminent domain. Our Constitution has clearly held that the payment of tax is distinct from the compulsory acquisition of property and has made independent provisions providing protection against taxation without authority from law. When Dr. Tekchand was asked if this was not in the right position, he did not advance any reasoned or convincing answer to refute his conclusion. In our opinion, the protection against imposition and collection of tax without authority of law comes directly from Article 265 and is not protected by Article (1) of Article 3. Article 265, not being in Chapter III of the Constitution, does not provide for its protection as a fundamental right which can be enforced by an application to this Court under Article 32. It is not intended to say that the right protected by Article 265 cannot be enforced. It can certainly be exercised by taking appropriate measures. What we mean to say is that this application in so far as it is intended to be established in this Court by Article 32 relating to Article 31 (1) is misconceived and will certainly fail.
Dr. Tekchand's entire argument was founded on the premise that protection against imposition and collection of tax without authority of law was guaranteed by Article 31 (1) and his attempt was to hold that the PEPSU ordinances were and could not, or could not, be made in law on a correct interpretation of them. to impose any income tax retrospectively, that the income tax officer had wrongly assessed the tax on the income earned before August 20, 1948, in an incorrect view of the law, and thereby threatened to deprive the applicant of property instead of the authority of the law. The view we have taken, namely, that protection against impposition or collection of tax is protected by Article 265 and not by Article 31 (2), does not really arise from the questions raised by Dr. Podchand and they need not express any opinion in this application. These questions can only be raised in appropriate proceedings and not in an application under Article 32. In our judgment this application failed on the general ground that no fundamental right of the applicant under Article 14 or Article 31 (1) has been infringed and we therefore dismissed the application with costs.
The appeal was rejected.
