Constitution of India, Articles 1 3, 6 (4), 29 (2), 46 - Admission to Educational Institutions - Executive order determining the number of seats for certain communities - Invalidity - Fundamental right against discrimination solely on the basis of religion - Directive Principles of State Policy - Value.
With regard to the admission of students to engineering and medical colleges in the state, the state of Maharashtra had issued an order (known as the Communal Government) that said the seats should be filled by the Selection Committee strictly on the following basis, i.e., 6 out of every 10 seats were allotted to non-Brahmins (Hindus), 2 to backward Hindus, 2 to Brahmins, 3 to Harijans, 1 each to Anglo-Indians and Indian Christians and 1 to Muslim.
The Communal Government constituted by the Full Court Order (Chief Justice Kanji, Fazal Ali, Ramachanal Shetty, Meherchanal Mahajan, Maneer Jeeves, and Devin Bose) violated the fundamental right guaranteed to the citizens of India by Article 29 (2) of the Constitution, namely, "No citizen shall be deprived of admission to, or of receiving aid out of, any educational institution maintained by the state on only ground of religion, race, caste, language or any of them," and hence struck down under Article 13 (1).
The Directive Principles of State Policy laid down in Part II of the Constitution cannot in any way superannuate or abridge the Fundamental Rights guaranteed by Part III. On the other hand, they have to be carried out in a manner consistent with and ancillary to the Fundamental Rights laid down in part III.

Under the heading "Cultural and Educational Rights" in Part III of the Constitution, Article 29 states as follows:
"(1) Any section of citizens residing in the territory of India or any part of it having a distinct language, script or culture of its own shall have the right to preserve it.
(2) No citizen shall, on ground only of religion, race, caste, or language, or any one of them, be deprived of admission to any educational institution maintained by the state or of receiving aid out of state funds.
It will be observed that while Clause (1) protects the language, script or culture of a section of citizens, Clause (2) guarantees the fundamental rights of an individual citizen. The right of admission to an educational institution of the type mentioned in Clause (2) is a right which a citizen has as a citizen and not as any member of the citizens. A citizen cannot be deprived of this right solely on the ground of religion, race, caste, or language. If a citizen who wishes to be admitted to any such educational institution does not possess the requisite educational qualifications and is
he is denied admission to the community or class on that ground, but he must not be heard to complain of a violation of his fundamental rights under this article. But, on the other hand, if he has an educational qualification but is denied admission solely on account of religion, race, caste, or language, or any of these, there will be a clear violation of His fundamental rights.
The learned Advocate-General, appearing for the state, contends that the provisions of this article must be read with other Articles of the Constitution. He pleads that Article 46 imposes upon the state to promote with special care the educational and economic interests of the weaker sections of the people, and in particular, the Scheduled Castes and Tribes, and to protect them from social injustice and exploitation. It is pointed out that though this article finds a place in Part II of the Constitution which lays down certain directive principles of state policy, and although the provisions contained in that Part are not enforceable by any court, the principles laid down therein make it compulsory on the part of the state to apply these principles to the governance of the fundamental country and to the enactment of Article 37. The argument is that having regard to the provisions of Article 46, the state retains the communal G.O. entitlement and fixes proportionate seats for the different communities. And if by reason of that order, which is thus claimed to be valid in law and not violative of the Constitution, the petitioners are unable to be admitted to educational institutions, there is no violation of any of their fundamental rights. In fact, the Learned Advocate-General of Malaysia has even contended that the sections of Article 46 override the provisions of Part 29 (2). We reject the above-mentioned contentions in their entirety. The directive principle of State policy, which has been expressly rendered unenforceable by a court by Article 37, cannot override any of the provisions found in Part III, which, notwithstanding other provisions, have been made expressly enforceable by appropriate writs, orders or directives under Article 32. The chapter on Fundamental Rights is sacrosanct and is not liable to be abridged by any law or executive act or order, except to the extent provided for in the appropriate paragraph of Part III. The directive principles of State policy must be carried out in a manner consistent with and ancillary to the chapter on Principles. This, in our opinion, is the proper way in which the laws found in Parts III and IV are to be understood. "(4) nothing in this article shall prevent the state from making any provision for the appointment to posts in favour of any backward class of citizens which, in the opinion of the state, is not adequately represented in the services under the state."
Had the arguments founded on Article 46 been correct, then clause (4) of Article 1 6 would have been wholly unnecessary. However, seeing that this clause (4) has been inserted in Article 1 6, the omission of such an express provision from Article 29 cannot be considered significant. It may well be that the purpose of the Constitution was not to introduce communal considerations at all in the matter of admission to any educational institution administered by the state or in the case of receiving aid out of state funds. The protection of the backward classes of citizens may require the appointment of members of those classes in state services, and in those circumstances it may be understood why the state has been empowered to reserve such appointments for the backward class. It is argued that applicants are not denied admission merely because they are Brahmins but there are various reasons for doing so, such as, (a) they areBrahmins, (b) they are allotted only two seats out of 10 and (c) two seats have already been filled by more meritorious Brahmin candidates. In this view of the matter, we do not consider it necessary to consider the implications of Articles 1 4 or 1 5 in the specific paragraphs discussed above.
