Constitution of India, 1949, Articles 14, 16 - Civil servants - Appointment on a contract for 5 years - Continuance of appointment on a temporary service basis - Termination of service on one month's notice - Validity - Fundamental rights - Civil Service (Temporary) Rules, 1949, Part 5.
The petitioner was employed by the Government of India in the Directorate of Rehabilitation and Employment, Ministry of Labour, on a contract of five years. When his contract was due to expire, the Government made a fresh offer to him to continue in his post temporarily for the term of the Rehabilitation and Employment Agency on the condition that he would be governed by the Central Civil Services (Temporary) Service Rules 1949, which provided for termination of the contract by one month's notice from both the parties. He accepted the offer and continued in service, but subsequently his service was terminated after giving him one month notice. The petitioner applied for redress under Article 32 (1) of the Constitution on the ground that his fundamental rights under Articles 311, 13 and 16 (1) of Constitution had been violated.

The judgment of the court was delivered on March 13, 1953.
Justice.- A petition under Article 32 of the Constitution in which the petitioner seeks, in his opinion, a remedy for the violation of his fundamental rights under Articles 7 and 16 (1) of the Constitution. It was argued at considerable length by the petitioner in person. Then, when our judgment was almost ready, he made an application for a fresh hearing and for permission to file some fresh papers. When this was refused he came again the next day and sought an application to appoint an agent and appeared through counsel as he felt that he was not able to judge the case in person. (It may be mentioned that he had originally engaged with an agent, though he appeared in person and before the hearing he dismissed him.) We granted his request and the counsel re-argued the case in his favour but did not proceed further. The facts are that.
In October, 1945, the applicant was appointed by the Government of India to the Directorate of Rehabilitation and Employment, Ministry of Labour, on a five-year contract. This was after being selected by the Federal Public Service Commission. After a short period of practical training, he was posted to Jabalpur in January 1946 as manager of the Sub-Regional Employment Exchange and was later confirmed in this appointment.
This contract of service was to expire in 1950. Shortly before the expiry, the Government of India made him a fresh offer, stated in the letter dated June 30, 1950, to continue his service after the expiry of his contract on the conditions stated in that letter. Among them were the following:
"(3) Other conditions of service: - After the termination of your contract you will be allowed to continue your post temporarily for the term of the Rehabilitation and Employment Agency and will be regulated by the Central Civil Services (Temporary) Regulations, 1949, till the time you become a permanent government servant."
In the letter he was asked to inform the Ministry of Labour whether he was willing to continue to work under these conditions and he admitted that he had accepted the consent and continued to work. He was not a permanent public servant, although the argument stated that he was, because he was on a five-year contract and the work for which he was employed, such as rehabilitation and employment, was only of a temporary character. The temporary work rules therefore apply.
In these rules, rule 5 is important. It is performed as follows:
"5. (a) The employment of a temporarily employed public servant who is not in semi-permanent employment shall be liable to termination at any time by the public servant at any time, by notice in writing given to the appointing authority, or by the appointment authority to the public servant.
(b) The duration of such notice shall be one month, unless otherwise agreed by the Government and the public servant."
Semi-permanent service is defined in the rules and it is clear that the applicant does not fall within that category. It is also an undisputed fact that there was no agreement between the applicant and the Government as to the period of notice. Hence as per this rule, which was a condition of the applicant's further contract of service, his services may be terminated at any time by one month's notice in writing. This notice was dated November 25, 1950 and he was told that his services would cease on the expiry of one month from December 1, 1950.
A large area was covered during the course of the debate, and if the matter had not been re-argued, we would not have dealt with the controversies raised more fully than the counsel who has now appeared.
The petition is under Article 32 (1) of the Constitution and therefore it must be shown that a fundamental right has been violated. It was argued that the rights violated are those provided by Articles 4 and 6 (1).
If Article 14 is taken first, it must show that the applicant has been discriminated against in the exercise or enjoyment of certain legal rights that are open to others similarly situated. The rights that he says have been violated are those provided for by Article 311. He states that he has been dismissed or removed from employment without the protections that this article provides. In our opinion, Article 311 has no application.
This is because it does not entail dismissal or removal from service, nor does it entail reduction in rank. It is common that a contract is being terminated by notice under one of its clauses.
The services in India have long provided certain statutory guarantees and protections against arbitrary dismissal or reduction in rank. Under Section 240 of the Government of India Act, 1935, the protections were limited to those two areas. Under the current Constitution, a third was added, namely, removal from service. To understand the difference between "dismissal" and "removal" from service, one must go back to the rules that governed "services" in India due to Article 313 of the Constitution and still govern with changes.
Part XII of the Civil Services (Classification, Registration and Appeal) Rules on Conduct and Discipline includes Rule 49 which sets out the various penalties that members of the services may be subject to for indiscriminate or misconduct. They are seven in number and include censure, dismissal, reduction of rank, removal from service and dismissal from service. The 1935 Act chose only two of these possible punishments as sufficiently serious to merit statutory protection, namely reduction in rank and dismissal of service. The Constitution added a third to the list. The distinction drawn between the two is explained in Rule 49. The first to be removed from service is "that which does not disqualify from future employment" and the next to be dismissed from service is 'that which ordinarily disqualifies from future employment."
Then there follows an explanation.
"Shedding"
(c) a person employed under the contract is not, in accordance with the terms of his contract, the result of removal or dismissal within the meaning of this rule.
These terms are used in the same sense as in Article 311. It follows that the paragraph has no application here and therefore there is no question of discrimination, that the "law" for which the applicant seeks its protection has no application.
There was no obligation on the applicant to enter into the contracts he had entered into. He was as free to accept or refuse what was offered to him as any other person under the law. On acceptance, he still left open to him all the rights and remedies available to other persons similarly situated to exercise any right which had been denied to him under his contract, presuming that he had a right to seek in the ordinary courts for any breach and to remedy it to the same extent as other persons similarly open to him. He was not discriminated against and was not denied the protection of any law which might be claimed by others similarly situated. The remedy of a writer is misconceived.
Section 16 (1) is not equally applicable. The whole point remains in the contract. When the applicant's first contract (of five years) expired, he was not a permanent public servant and the government was not obliged to re-employ him or continue him in service. On the other hand, the government was open to offering him temporary and contractual employment to continue. Although the employment continued, it was, in practice and in the eyes of the law, under a new and different contract which was entirely different and distinct from the old one although many of its conditions remained the same. Section 16 (1) deals with equality of opportunity in all matters relating to employment or appointment in any office subordinate to the state. The applicant has not been denied any opportunity of employment or appointment. He has been treated in the same manner as any other person who was offered temporary employment under these conditions. His complaint, when analysed, is not of personal distinction but is against the offer of temporary employment under special conditions as opposed to permanent employment. Of course, however, the state may enter into contracts of temporary employment and impose special conditions in each case, provided they are not inconsistent with the Constitution, and those who choose to accept these conditions and enter into contracts are bound by them, even if the state is bound by them. When employment is permanent there are certain statutory guarantees but in the absence of these there are exceptions.
Any such limitation, subject to the qualifications mentioned above, is free to enter into special contracts of service with temporary employees engaged in work of a temporary nature, as with any other employer.
Various matters relating to the merits of the case have been mentioned but we express no opinion as to whether the applicant has any other right which he can exercise by other means. We are here dealing with a suit under Article 32 for the enforcement of a fundamental right and we decide that the only issue is that no fundamental right has been violated.
When the matter was first argued, we decided that we would not pass any order as to costs, but now that the petitioner is adamantly insistent on reopening the case and has called the learned Attorney-General here for the second time, we have no alternative but to dismiss the petition with costs.
The petition was rejected.
