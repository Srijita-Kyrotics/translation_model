Vs
of the State of Delhi and others.
[Judges - Patanjali Sastri সি.জে., Mukerjea, এস.আর.Das, Ghulam Hassan and Bhagwati.]
Criminal trial - Adjournment of trial - Defendant not ordered to be remanded in custody - Validity of detention - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1989, Section 3 4 4 - Habeas corpus.
In habeas corpus proceedings, the court must consider the legality or otherwise of the detention at the time of return and not with reference to the institution of the proceedings.
Section 3 4 4 of the Code of Criminal Procedure requires a magistrate, if he wishes to adjourn a case, to remand the accused by warrant if he is in custody "and further provides that every order passed by any court other than the High Court under this section must be in writing. Where a judicial magistrate had adjourned a case by order in writing but there was nothing on record to show that he had ordered custody of the accused.
Order: - The accused was illegally detained after the order of adjournment.
Those who call upon other persons to deprive them of their personal liberty, which they consider to be their duty, must strictly and scrupulously observe the forms and rules of the law.
Original Judiciary; Petition No. 54 of 1953.
Petition under Article 32 of the Constitution for a writ in the nature of habeas corpus.
Jay Gopal Shetty and Ved Vyas (S. K. Kapoor, A. K. Dutta, A. N. Chona, R. Pathnayak and A. N. Sinha, with them) for the petitioners.
C. K. Office, Solicitor-General of India for the respondents (Porus A. Mehta, with him).
1959 March 3. Judgment of the Court is delivered by the Chief Justice.
Patanjali Shastri সি.যে. - This is Ram Narayan Singh by one Ram Narayan Singh of four gentlemen, namely, Dr. এস.পি.Mukherjee, Shri এন.সি.Chatterjee, Pandit Nandalal Sharma and Pandit Guru Datt Vaid, who are the petitioners in the original case.
The men were arrested on the evening of March 6, 1953, and are now being tried for allegedly disobeying orders banning meetings and processions in the area in question, an offence punishable under Section 18 of the Indian Penal Code.
Their detention has been sought to be justified on the basis of two remand orders, one allegedly passed by Mr. Dhillon, Additional District Magistrate, Delhi; at about 8 p.m. on March 6, 1953, and the other on March 9, 1953, at about 3 p.m., where it is alleged to have been passed by the Judicial Magistrate. A habeas corpus petition before him has been filed before this Court during the pendency of the case.
Mr. Sethi, for the petitioner, has put before us various questions of law and facts, but we find it unnecessary to enter into a discussion of those questions, as it is now admitted that the first order of remand was passed on March 6. Even assuming that it was a valid one expired on March 9 and is no longer in force. The stand of the remand order which the trial magistrate is alleged to have passed on March 9, is as follows: - The trial magistrate was evidently proceeding at that stage under Section 344 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, which, if necessary, he decides to adjourn the case pending before him, "to take the accused on remand by warrant if in custody," and provides that: Every order made under this section by any court other than the High Court shall be signed in writing, by the presiding judge or magistrate. The order of the magistrate under this section was presented to us in concurrence with the order of this court made on March 19, which directs that the records before the additional district magistrate and the judge magistrate be produced before this court as soon as possible. Along with the remand papers for inspection by counsel for the petitioner. The order as produced only directs that the case be kept pending till March 11 and there is no direction to keep the accused in custody till that date.
Last evening, four slips of paper were handed over to the registrar of this court at 5-2 0. On the one hand they carried a warrant of detention dated March 6 and an order addressed to the jail superintendent of Delhi to keep the accused in judicial lock-up and produce him before the court on March 9,1953. These warrants have the following sanction behind them: judicial remand till March 11,1953. "
On a question of habeas corpus, when the validity or otherwise of the custody of the persons concerned is called into question, it is clear that these documents, if indeed essential, were not produced, despite the clear instructions in our order dated March 19. There is no order of remand dated March 9 in the court records produced before us. As we have already noted, the case has been adjourned to the 11th by order of the Judicial Magistrate. The Solicitor-General, appearing for the Government, explained that these slips of paper, which would be of great importance to the case, were held by a police officer who was present in the court yesterday, but after the court rose in the evening the next thought was that something they had produced might be of importance and so they were filed before the Registrar on 5-2 0. Me We cannot take notice of the documents produced in such circumstances, and we are not satisfied that there was an order of remand for further custody of the accused until March 11. It has been held by this court that in a habeas corpus proceeding, the court has to consider the validity or otherwise of the detention at the time of return and not by reference to the institution of the proceedings. The material date for the facts in this case is March 19, when the affidavit was filed on behalf of the government that the detention was proved to be lawful. But the position, as we have said, was that there was no order for custody of the four persons on that date. This has often been reiterated before this court by those who have
feels called upon to deprive other persons of their personal liberty, which they deem to be their duty, to observe strictly and scrupulously the form and rules of the law. In this case it has not been done. The petitioners before us now are therefore entitled to be released, and they are released forthwith.
The petition has been allowed.
