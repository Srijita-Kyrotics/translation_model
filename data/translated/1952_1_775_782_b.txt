Bihar Tenancy Act, 1937, s. 5 (2), 20-21 - "Juripesh" Lease - Lease by mortgagee for a term of 3 years - Lease in possession for more than 30 years - Right of acquisition - To take possession - Mortgagee's power to lease - Limitations - Transfer of property Act, (IV of 1882), s. 76 (a) and (e).
As a general rule, a person cannot transfer or otherwise assign a title better than his own to another, and a mortgagor cannot therefore create an interest in the mortgaged property which his interest will ensure even after the termination of the mortgage. Moreover, the landlord cannot in any way do anything detrimental to the interest of the landlord during the subsistence of the mortgagee's law, such as by granting a lease which may enable the tenant to acquire a right of possession of the land, thereby forfeiting the landlord's right of pawn.
The emergence of a tenancy right granted by a tenant to a tenant by prudent management, based on the nature of the land and the time required, or created by law, is an exception to the general rule. But falling within this exception the tenancy settlement by the tenant must be a fair and proper one. The exception shall not apply where the terms of the mortgage expressly permit the tenant from making any settlement on the land, either by the mortgagee or by the required money.
Where a "jurisdiction" lease deed contains the following conditions; "Let the lessees enter into the possession and possession of the part of the land left in the lease (being the Kudakhat land under their own cultivation), cultivate them, pay the rent retained year after year to us, the executors, and make suitable arrangements for the produce. "Year after year" due to the tenant having an interest in the quantity and the quality of the work performed by the tenant to whom the lands were leased by mortgage for three years refers to the land deed and there is a clear provision that they (the tenants) will relinquish possession of the "tika" land when the lease expires without claiming the land as their own.
Order: - The decision of the High Court confirms that the settlement is not bona fide and correct and that the successors (defendants) of the tenants have not acquired a right of possession of the dead lands under the Bihar Tenancy Act though the lands were in the possession of the lessee and his heirs for more than 30 years after the expiry of the lease period.
The order further said that the defendants could not have acquired the right of possession under sections 20 and 21 of the Bihar Tenancy Act because the mortgagee is the "property holder" or the "term holder" and not the tenant and his heirs, therefore, "Section 5, paragraph (2), of the said Act is disposed of."

The emergence of tenancy rights granted to a tenant by a tenant through prudent management is an entirely different matter based on the nature of the settlement and the land for the period required by the tenant.
