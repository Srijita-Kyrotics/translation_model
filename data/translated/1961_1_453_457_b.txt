Income Tax Act - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922 (II of 1922 )'B'as amended by the Indian Income Tax (Amendment) Act (25 of 1953) 14 (2) (C) - Income Tax in Part States - Applicability Clause.
The appellant, a private limited company, was incorporated in 1954 in the erstwhile Kotah State which was merged with the United States of Rajasthan in 1949. The State of Rajasthan became the State of Rajasthan, a'B'-part State. The Indian Finance Act, 1950, made the Indian Income-tax Act, 1922, applicable to the'B'-part States with effect from April 1, 1950, thereby making Rajasthan a taxable territory. The Income-tax (Amendment) Act, 1953 was amended. The Income-tax authorities then sought to impose a tax on the profits and income of the appellant for the assessment year, who claimed an exemption under Section 14 (2) (c) of the Indian Income-tax Act, 1922, as it existed before the amendment in 1953. DECISION The question for this was whether the argument was still open to the appellant in view of the decision of this Court in Madan Gopal's case that the amendment was effective from April 1, 1950, and whether the income earned before April 1, 1950, would still be exempt, even though the exemption had been withdrawn only from April 1, 1950.
DECISION: Withdrawal of exemption in the assessment year 1950-51 has adversely affected the income of the previous year 1949-50.The application of the Indian Income Tax Act made Rajasthan a taxable territory subject to the Indian Income Tax Act conditions, and Parliament was able to enact a new law for the territory, just as it did for the whole of India.
Section 14 (2) (C) is introduced in the amendment. 1949-50 The exemption in respect of liability to tax on income for the year disappears as if it had never been granted and the exemption is deleted. The whole intention and intent of the amendment was to arrive at this result from the assessment year 1950-51, and there can be no repeal. The argument assumes the premise that the Income-tax Act was incorporated in the Indian Finance Act, 1950, but there is no precedent for the idea, or for the assumption, that when one Act applies another law in a territory, the latter must be incorporated in the former. It may also be otherwise, if there is a wording which shows that the former law is deemed to have been re-enacted by the new law.

The appellant is a private limited company, incorporated in 1945 in the erstwhile Kota State. The income tax authorities sought to tax its profits and income for the 1950-51 assessment year in relation to the previous year, 1949-50. The appellant claimed exemption under Section 14 (2) (c) of the Indian Income Tax Act, 1922, as it existed prior to the 1953 amendment, contending that the exemption was still applicable after the amendment. This claim was rejected by the High Court, which was raised under Article 226 of the Constitution. Hence this petition.
Prior to the unification of the State of Kotah with the United State of Rajasthan in 1949, no income tax law was in force in the State of Kotah. Before the formation of the State of Rajasthan no such law was in force in any part of Rajasthan except the State of Bundi. The Indian Finance Act of 1950 made the Indian Income Tax Act, 1922 applicable to the whole of India except the State of Jammu and Kashmir and duly amended the Indian Income Tax Act. Rajasthan became a taxable territory with effect from 1st April 1950.
For the assessment year 1950-51, an attempt was made to impose income tax in the state of Rajasthan. one.

Madan Gopal Kabra moved the High Court under Article 226 of the Constitution to restrain the tax authorities from claiming tax for the period prior to April 1, 1950, contending that since Rajasthan was not a taxable territory before April 1, 1950, no tax could be claimed for the period prior to that date. It is not necessary to give details of the decision on that occasion. The judgment of this Court is published in The Union of India v. Madan Gopal Kabra (1).
The appellants and fourteen others present appealed under Article 226 of the Constitution, claiming new grounds by way of a subsequent amendment. Their argument was that Section 14 (2) (c) was exempted by the Indian Income-tax Act on April 1, 1950, and that the exemption was not affected by the amendment of the provision referred to earlier in 1953, although the amendment was retroactive from April 1, 1950, unless the Finance Act, 1950, which applied the Income-tax Act in this case, was amended. This contention was not accepted by the High Court which dismissed the appeal under Article 226, holding, inter alia, that the same had been decided by this Court against Madan Gopal Kabra.
In this appeal, only this point has been disputed, and it has been argued that this issue is still open for decision. Section 14 (2) (c), which was there before the 1953 amendment, reads as follows;

It may be mentioned here that the tax exemption under Section 14 (2) (c) of the Indian Income Tax Act, accrued between the States in Part B. Except in the case of the State of Jammu and Kashmir, by way of amendment of that provision with effect from the first day of April, 1950.
Mr. N. C. Chatterjee, appearing for the appellant, contended that the matter could not be considered to have been conclusively decided and that the remark was only descriptive of what Parliament intended to do. He contended that the matter could and should be reconsidered. In support of his argument, he pleaded that the effect of the passing of the Indian Finance Act, 1950 and the application of the Indian Income-tax Act to Rajasthan and other Part B States was to subsume the Indian Income-tax Act by relation as such changes and amendments to the Indian Finance Act were then made. No subsequent amendment to the Indian Income-tax Act had any effect on the substantive law incorporated by relation in the Indian Finance Act, unless the latter were also suitably amended. Kabra's case (1) which did not find support was raised again in another form. This amendment came into force on 1st April 1950 and the income earned before 1st April 1950 was still exempt, as the exemption was withdrawn only from 1st April 1950.
In our opinion, both arguments have no substance and the position indicated by this Court in the portion cited earlier, represents the true state of the law. Firstly, exemption is granted in respect of tax liability in any year of assessment and exemption is granted in respect of income of the previous year in the assessment year 1950-51. For the same reason, withdrawal of exemption in the assessment year 1950-51 has adversely affected the income of the previous year, which is the subject matter of this case.

The latter argument gives a wrong impression of the nature of the Indian Finance Act, 1950. By that Act, the Indian Income-tax Act was applied, but the Income-tax Act was not incorporated by relation into the Indian Finance Act. The application of the Indian Income-tax Act made Rajasthan a taxable territory subject to the Indian Income-tax Act, and Parliament was competent to make a new law for the territory, just as it did for the whole of India. The exemption raised by the amendment disappeared as if it had never been granted, and if there was no repeal the amendment must serve to eliminate the exemption. In fact, the whole intention and intent of the amendment was to arrive at this result from the assessment year 1950-51, and there can be no repeal. The argument assumes the premise that the Income-tax Act was incorporated in the Indian Finance Act, 1950, but there is no precedent or presumption for the notion that when one law applies another law to a territory, the latter must be incorporated into the former. It may also be otherwise, if there is a wording which shows that the former law is deemed to have been re-enacted by the new law. The Indian Finance Act, 1950, in this case was concerned with the application of the Indian Income-tax Act, which it amends the definition of'taxable territory'in the Indian Income-tax Act, and applies that law to this territory. Thereafter, the Indian Parliament may retroactively amend the Income-tax Act, and the amendment shall also apply to the new taxable territory. In our opinion, neither argument is valid.
The appeal was unsuccessful and will be dismissed with costs.
The appeal was turned down.
