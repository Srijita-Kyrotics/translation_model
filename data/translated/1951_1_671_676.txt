Manohar Lal Vs.
[Chief Justice Sri Ramalinga, Justice Syed Fazal, Justice Meher Khan, Justice Chandrashekhar Iyer and Justice Vivian Bose]
Punjab Commercial Employees Act, 1940, Section 2-A (i) & (j), 7 (1), 16 - Shopkeeper without employee - Sale by owner on closing day - Liability of shopkeeper - Scope of Section 2 (a) (i) and (j).
Section 7 and paragraph (1). The Punjab Industrial Employees' Bill, 1940, as amended in 1943, provides that "except as otherwise provided by this Act, every shop shall remain closed on a day of closure." Subsection (2) (i) states that "the decision as to the day on which the shop shall be closed shall lie with the owner or occupier of the shop and the same shall be notified to the prescribed authority."
Nothing in the Act shall apply to the persons appointed in the managing capacity and the family members of the employer in terms of clauses (i) and (j) of the latter in section 2A. The appellant was the owner of a shop, and on a closing day the son of the appellant sold an article from the shop, and the appellant was convicted under section 16 of the Act. It was contended on his behalf that section 7 of the Act was ultra vires (superior) as it did not fall within any of the clauses of the Provincial or Combined Legislative List of the Government of India, 1935, and in any case he did not employ any worker and was the director (manager) of the shop, therefore in terms of the provisions of clauses (6) and (ii) of the said Act.
He cannot be convicted of a crime.
It was unanimously decided by the court (i) that the provincial government could regulate the time, place, date and rules of sale of an item under Clause No. 27 of List II and Clause 7 of the Act was not ultravires (ultravires); the matter could be brought under Clause 27 "Conditions of Welfare of the Working Man;" of List III.
(ii) Clause (j) of Section 2A- did not protect the appellant as he was not convicted for the sale by the son but for keeping the shop open on the day of the appellant's closure;
(iii) He was not entitled to be discharged under clause (i) of section 2A despite the fact that the appellant himself was the director (manager) of the shop, as his powers and responsibilities as an owner have to be kept separate from that of a manager for the purpose of law.

1951. May 23. The judgment of the court was delivered.
By Justice Bose --- This is against the conviction under Section 7 (1) of the Punjab Civil Employees Act, 1940, as amended in 1943, a criminal appeal.
The appellant is a shopkeeper who owns and operates a shop in the Cantonal area of Ferozepur. He has no "employee" within the meaning of this Act but is assisted by his son in running the shop. The shop is divided into two sections. In one, haberdashery items are sold; in the other, stationery items.
Section 7 (1) of the amended Act provides that "unless otherwise provided by this Act, every shop shall remain closed on ....................................................................................................................................................................................................................................................................................... ................................................_______...................................................................................... ................................................................................... ................................. ................................ ........................... ................... ........................................ ................ ................................. ...................................... ................................... ... ........................ ................................. .........................................
Subsection (2) (i) provides that-
"The choice of an off day will lie with the owner or occupier of a shop... and that has to be notified to the prescribed authority etc.."
The appellant preferred the following. He chose to close the haberdashery section on Mondays and the stationery section on Saturdays and gave the necessary initiation to the concerned authorities.
On Monday, May 17, 1948, the appellant's son sold a tin of boot polish from the haberdashery section of the shop to a consumer. The appellant was personally present during the sale. Monday was a holiday for the haberdashery section and therefore the appellant was charged under sections 7 and 16 of the Act. The Magistrate was holding that the sale of haberdahsery goods on a holiday violated the provisions of Section 7 (1) of the Act in not observing Monday as the appealant's holiday. Accordingly, he convicted him and imposed a fine of Rs 20. A revision ('revision') application to the High Court was refused. In the judgment, the High Court held that since the appellant had failed to keep his shop closed once a week, his conviction was correct. The certificate of leave to appeal ('leave to appeal') was granted to this court on the ground that it involved a substantial question of law relating to the Government of India, 1935, and thus the matter has come to us.
The learned counsel for the petitioner contended that Section 7 of the Act is ultra-vires as it does not fall in any matter under the Provincial or Combined Law List of the Government of India, 1935. In our opinion, the matter may come under Clause No. 27 of List II or Clause No, 27 of List III.
Item No. 27 of List II explains "Trade and Commerce within the Province." In our opinion, a provincial government may, under that entry, "regulate the time, place, date and manner of sale of any particular goods or commodities. It (the provincial government) may, for example, state that the sale of explosives or other dangerous substances should take place only in selected areas, at specified times or on specified days when extra precautions may be taken for the general safety of the public and those directly concerned. Clearly, it can certainly. Similarly, it may, if it is pleased, state that there shall be no sale on a particular day, whether Sunday or Friday, or on religious or cultural days and so on. Instead of doing that, it has chosen to regulate the internal trade of the province in this manner as just one of the various ways in which it may act.
The matter may also be brought under the subject "Labourer's Welfare and Working Conditions;" in point 27 of List III. The proposed clause is a general clause and applies to all types of shops; that is, those in which workers are employed as well as those run by the owners and their family members. Where this clause is contained in this Act, it is directed to regulate the working hours of persons engaged in the business of shops or commercial establishments. Therefore, in so far as section 7 covers establishments employing workers, it is undoubtedly 'internal'. " But it was argued that this clause could have no application in cases where shops are run by an owner with or without the assistance of his family. For this, reliance was placed on clauses (i) and (j) in section 2 which read as follows:
"2 -- A. Nothing in this Act shall apply to-
* * * * **
(i) the person appointed in a managing capacity... and
(j) Family members of the employer."
It was argued that the sale was made by the son. He is not affected by the law. Therefore, he was entitled to sell and could not sell unless he kept the shop open to sell. To enable him to do so. The same applies in the case of the appellant, the owner, who was there in the capacity of a manager. In our view, this is wrong because the conviction here is not for sale but for keeping the shop open on the day of the closure. Section 2 (j) does not give the son the right to keep the shop open, or, in that case, the right to sell. It simply states that, being a member of the family, he shall not be affected by the provisions of the law. Section 7 (1) of this Act is directed against the owner of the shop, not against his family. It obliges the owner to keep his shop closed once a week.
It was then argued that if a person appointed in a managerial capacity could not be affected by the Act, the appellant in that capacity could not have been compelled to close the shop under section 7 (1). This is also wrong. It has happened in the present case that the owner and the manager are the same person but the Act clearly provides for a class of events in which they are different. The owner is obliged to open the shop once a week, although the manager of the shop, for example, may work without the continuous twenty-four hours of rest per week provided for in section 7. The appellant's power as manager is to be distinguished for this purpose from his character as owner. Section 2A (i) does not control sections 7 (1) and (2).
Lastly, it was argued that in simple terms the purpose of the Act is to improve the conditions of the workers employed in the shop. It cannot therefore apply to shops in which no worker is employed, especially when the "employer's" family is expressly excluded from the scope of the Act. For this reason alone, it cannot fall under clause 27 of List 3. We hold that such a narrow interpretation cannot be placed on the subject. In order to minimise the possibility of a minimum loophole, it has therefore been felt necessary by the legislature to interfere with the liberty of those who have not been otherwise affected. We hold that they had the power to do so. Further, to say that a shopkeeper, who employs one or two people, is required to keep his shop closed, and to allow his competitors, who employ perhaps a dozen members of his family, to keep it (the shop) open, clearly puts the former at a serious commercial disadvantage. To allow such a distinction may give rise to disaffection, and ultimately to a relationship between the employer and the workers. These are all matters of principle into which we cannot enter but which the words and phrases of all these matters support a broad and liberal interpretation.
The appeal was unsuccessful and was dismissed.
The appeal is rejected.
