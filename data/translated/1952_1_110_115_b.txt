Harla
Vs.
State of Rajasthan
[Justice Meher Chand Mahajan and Justice Vivian Bose]
Jaipur Rules Act, 1923, Clause.3 (b) - Jaipur Opium Act, 1923 - The Act issued by the Council of Ministers has not been published in the Gazette - Validity of the Act - Necessity to issue the Act is natural justice.
Natural justice requires that any law must be published before it comes into force. It must be broadcast in some recognizable way so that all men may know what it is; or at least there must be some special rule or regulation or customary channel by or through which such knowledge may be acquired with the application of due and reasonable diligence.
The State Council of Ministers, appointed by the Crown Representative for the Government and Administration of Jaipur, passed a resolution in 1923 to enact a law in the name of the Jaipur Opium Act, but this Act was neither issued nor published in the Gazette nor communicated to the public. The Jaipur Law Act, 1923, which was also passed by the Council and which came into force on November 1, 1924, provided by Section 3 (b) that the law to be administered by the courts of the State of Jaipur shall be.. "(b) All regulations now in force within the said territories and such laws and regulations as may be issued from time to time thereafter shall be published in the State, and the Official Gazette." In 1938 the Jaipur Opium Act was amended by adding a clause stating that "it shall come into force on September 1, 1924."
It is ordered that the passing of the resolution of the Council without further publication or introduction of the Act was not sufficient to give effect to the Act and the Jaipur Opium Act was, therefore, not a valid Act. It is further held that the said Act was not preserved by Section 3 (b) of the Jaipur Rules Act, since it was not a valid Act in force on the 1st day of November, 1924 and adding a clause in 1938 that it would come into force in 1924 was of no use.

Judge Bose - The applicant was found guilty under Section 7 of the Jaipur Opium Act and fined Rs. 50. The case is frivolous as it is but the High Court of Rajasthan at Jaipur has granted special leave to appeal as an important matter touching upon the provisions of the Act. We shall narrate the facts chronologically. It is admitted that the rulers of Jaipur had full powers of government, including legislative. On September 7, 1922, the late Maharaja passed away and at the time of his death his heir, the present Maharaja, was a minor. Accordingly, the Crown Representative appointed a Council of Ministers to look after the government and administration of the State during the Maharaja's minor period.
On December 11, 1923, this Council passed a resolution calling for the enactment of the Jaipur Opium Act, and the only question is whether the mere passing of the resolution without publication or disclosure in the Gazette, or by other means of making the Act known to the public, was sufficient to make it law. We are of the opinion that it was not. But before giving our reasons for so holding, we shall refer to some further facts.
At about the same time (that is, we are not given the exact date in 1923) the same Council enacted the Jaipur Rules Act, 1923. Section 3 (b) of this Act is given as follows: ---
"3. the law administered by the courts of the State of Jaipur, subject to the prerogative of the ruler, shall be as follows:
(b) all regulations now in force within the said territories, and subsequently laws and regulations passed from time to time by the State and published in the official gazette. "This Act came into force on November 1, 1924.
It is admitted that the Jaipur Opium Act was not published in the Gazette before or after 1 November 1924. It was argued, however, that this was not necessary as it was a "regulation" which was already in force on that date.
The only other fact of the result is that on May 19, 1938, Section 1 of the Jaipur Opium Act was amended by the addition of sub-section (c) which read as follows:
"(c) It shall come into force on September 1, 1924."
The offence for which the appellant was convicted took place on 8 October 1948.
Dealing first with the last of these laws, that is, the one of May 19, 1938, we can set it aside one at a time because, as long as the opium law was valid when it was enacted, a condition that it would come into force on some date only fourteen years earlier than when it was added fourteen years later would be void. There was a law in 1938 that required all laws after November 1, 1924 to be published in the gazette. Therefore, if the opium law was not a valid law at that date, it could not have been validated by the publication of a single clause of it in the gazette fourteen years later. The Jaipur Act of 1923 required the entire act of the law to be published; therefore the publication of only one section would not legitimize it if it was not already valid. We need not consider whether a law can be made retroactive to take effect from 1924 by publication in 1938, although that argument was argued. It returns us to the position of 1923 and raises the question of whether a law can be enacted by a mere resolution of the Jaipur Council.
We do not know what law was in force in Jaipur with respect to the operation of a law in that State. We have not been shown any, nor have our attention been called to any custom which may be said to govern the matter. In the absence of any particular law or custom, we are of opinion that it would be contrary to the principles of natural justice to permit the subjects of a State to be punished or penalized by a law of which they had no knowledge and which they could not. Even with reasonable diligence any knowledge has been acquired. Natural justice requires that before the law comes into force it must be issued or published. It must be broadcast in some recognized way so that all men may know what it is; or, at least, there must be some special rule or regulation or customary channel by or through which such knowledge may be obtained with due and reasonable diligence. The idea that a room has been reached within the secret recesses of which the public have no access and to which even their acknowledged representatives have no access and to which they may generally know nothing, may nevertheless affect their life, liberty, and property. To pass a mere proposition without doing anything else is an abomination to the civilized people. It shocks its conscience. Therefore in the absence of any law, rule, regulation, or custom, we hold that a law cannot be made in this way. Some reasonable form of dissemination or publication is indispensable.
In England the rule is that Acts of Parliament become law from the first instant of the day on which they receive royal assent, but royal proclamations only when actually published in the official Gazette. See footnote (a) to Article 776, page 601, Laws of Halsbury, England (Hailsham Edition), Vol VI and 32 Halbury's Laws of England (Hailsham Edition), page 150 Note (r). But even there it was necessary for Parliament to enact a special Act to enable such proclamations to become law by publication in the Gazette although a royal proclamation is the highest form of legislation, other than an Act of Parliament known in the British Constitution; even publication in the London Gazette would not make the proclamation valid in Scotland nor publication in the Edinburgh Gazette legal for England. It is therefore clear that the enactment or signature of a royal proclamation alone is not sufficient. There must be publication before it becomes law, and the nature of publication in England must be determined by an Act of Parliament.
The Act of Parliament regulating this matter is the Crown Office Act 1877 (40 & 41 Victoria ch. 41). That Act, in addition to making provision for publication in some official gazettes, also made provision for the making of rules by Order in Council for the best means of making announcements known to the public. The British Parliament has, therefore, at the root of the Crown Office Act, insisted that there should not be publication only in newspapers, but that there should be other methods of publication in addition, if an Order in Council so directs, so that the general public may know what they are. Special Acts. The Crown Office Act carefully instructs His Majesty in Council to consider the best method of making these laws known to the public, and gives that body the power to make rules for it and to embody them in an Order in Council. We accept that these announcements would not be valid legislation if they were not published strictly in accordance with the rules so made.
The principle underlying this question has been judicially considered in England. On a somewhat lower plane, for example, it was held under the Johnson v. Sargant order, 1917, that an order of the Food Controller (1) does not take effect until the beans, peas and lentils have been made known to the public, and the distinction between such an order and an order is emphasised by the law of the British Parliament. The difference is obvious. The laws of the British Parliament are enacted in public. The debates are open to the public and the laws are passed by acknowledged representatives of the people who can theoretically be believed to know what their contents are made of. They receive wide circulation on paper and now by radio. Not so the royal proclamation and the order of the Food Controller, etc. There must therefore be circulation and publication in their case. The means of publication may vary; what is good procedure in one country may not be best in another. But there must be some form of publication that is reasonable.

It is true that none of these cases resemble the one before us but they are only special applications of a profound rule which is founded on natural justice.
The Council of Ministers which passed the Jaipur Opium Act was not a sovereign body nor did it act in its own right. It was brought into existence by the Crown Representative, and the Jaipur Gazette Notification dated August 11, 1923, defined and limited its powers. We are therefore entitled to import this point, in view of the principles and concepts of natural justice contained in the British Constitution, because it is inconceivable that a representative of His Britannic Majesty could think of creating a body which could possess such an abominable power. Fundamental principles of natural justice which are shared by all freedom-loving people. We hold that, in the absence of certain laws or customs, a mere proposal by the Council of Ministers of the State of Jaipur would no longer suffice to give effect to a law without publication or promulgation.
Another point needs to be considered. It was urged that Section 3 (b) of the Jaipur Act of 1923 protected all regulations of the time from the requirement of publication in the Gazette. That may be so, but the Act reserved only those laws which were valid at that time and not proposals which did not acquire the force of law.
The appeal is successful. The conviction and sentence are set aside. The fine, if paid, will be refunded.
